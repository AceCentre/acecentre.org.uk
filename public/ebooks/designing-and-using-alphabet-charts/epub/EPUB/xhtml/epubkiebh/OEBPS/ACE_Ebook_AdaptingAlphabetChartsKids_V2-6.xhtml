An individual may also require a range of tools that are flexible enough to enable them to vary how they communicate depending upon mood, energy levels and well-being. For example, someone might be able to point directly to an alphabet chart during the day, but in the evening may be tired and need to use an alternative approach such as listener mediated scanning.

If someone is using more than one type of accesss method to paper-based communication, it may affect the design of the resources. You will need to ensure that the resources can either accommodate both access methods, or, if you are using separate ones, that the resources are as consistent as possible. You may also want to think through how someone can request each tool. Information about this should be well documented, perhaps on the resource itself and / or in a Communication Passport.

Similarly, a need to communicate in more than one langauge may affect the design. A separate resource may be required for each language. Alternatively, depending upon the language concerned, the needs of both may be able to be met using a single chart.

On a very practical note, consider having multiple copies of any system around the place so that there is always something to hand. This is a strategy used by Tiago and his family. Some find it helpful to keep a copy in every room of the house; others find it helpful to keep one in a handbag, pocket, with their mobile phone and / or permanently attached to any wheelchair.
