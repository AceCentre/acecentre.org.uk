Anjali uses her alphabet chart to spell out entire words and phrases.

However, alphabet charts are sometimes used alongside speech if there are issues with clarity. Sometimes simply pointing to the first letter of the word is enough to cue a communication partner into what the individual is saying. Someone might also use this approach alongside another communication tools if they have early literacy skills and can identify the first letter of a word but find it difficult to spell the rest of the word.

Another strategy that can help to cue a communication partner into what is being said is to share the topic of conversation. See Chapter 10 for more information about this.

More information about letter and topic cueing can be found on the ALS Program area of the Boston Children’s Hospital website. They also have letter and topic cueing charts for available download.

To make an alphabet chart and / or a cueing chart into a robust communication tool, it is worth either laminating it or printing it on specialist paper. The laminate sometimes makes it easier for someone to slide their finger around on the chart.

Supporting the use of an Alphabet Chart if Pointing Accurately to Letters is a Little Difficult

If someone is finding pointing accurately to letters a little difficult, one option is to provide more detailed instructions or training to communication partners on how the chart is used. For example, a message along the top of the chart might say, “I use my index finger to select letters - ignore my other fingers”, which would help a communication partner know what to focus on.

Communication partners could also speak aloud or write down the letters as they are selected, so everyone knows they are on the right track. It is quickest if the person only has to let the communication partner know when they have got it wrong, rather than agreeing that they have got it right with each letter selected. This is the approach used by Suzanne when talking with Anjali in the video above.
