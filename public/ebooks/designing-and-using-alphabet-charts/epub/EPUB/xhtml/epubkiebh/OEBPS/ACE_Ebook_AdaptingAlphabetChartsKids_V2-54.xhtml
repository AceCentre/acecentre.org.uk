Chapter 9

Issues to Consider When Designing an Alphabet Chart

However it is being accessed, small changes to an alphabet chart can make a big difference to usability. It is therefore worth spending some time finding the right alphabet chart for the individual.

This chapter looks at the ways in which alphabet charts can differ and considers tweaks and modifications that can make them more effective.

Size of the Chart, Font Type, Colour and Size, and the Amount of Space Between Letters

Alphabet charts are often presented on A4 paper. However, there is no reason why other sizes shouldn’t be used. As discussed in relation to eye pointing charts, while the assumption is often that bigger is better, in fact some prefer a small chart. For charts accessed by listener mediated scanning or eye pointing, it may be about finding the size that is easiest both to hold and see.

As discussed in Chapter 2, the decision about size may be affected by someone’s physical skills, particularly if they are pointing directly to the chart. For someone with a limited range of movement, a smaller chart may be easier to access, while for someone with less refined movement, a larger chart may be easier to use.

The choice of font is down to personal preference, but it is worth making sure that it is as easy to read as possible. Children may be more familiar with lower case letters.

When producing a printed alphabet chart, people tend to use black font on a white background. However, there are times when other colour combinations can be useful or simply preferable.
