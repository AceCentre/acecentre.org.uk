An eye pointing communication chart - available for download from acecentre.org.uk On the eye pointing chart illustrated for example, the individual might use eye pointing to select the group containing the characters ‘a, b, c, 1, 2, 3’. The communication partner could then offer each character in turn within that group until they indicated ‘yes’ in an agreed way. To help develop the individual’s understanding of the encoding process, the communication partner could draw attention to the colour of each character as they offered it. For example they could offer, “yellow a, pink b, blue c, green 1, black 2, red 3”, perhaps even tapping the corresponding coloured dot as they said the colour.

﻿Tips for Communication Partners when using Eye Pointing

In the early stages, a communication partner may need to double check whether they have correctly ‘read’ the eye pointing. As discussed in Chapter 11 however, the communication partner should be wary of falling into the trap of asking someone to indicate ‘yes’ or ‘no’ to every letter they have selected. The individual may become frustrated by being required to continually repeat themselves and it can really slow communication down. Often it is quicker to rely upon the individual to let the communication partner know when they’ve got it wrong - for example by pulling a face, or looking away completely.

Rather than asking questions, it can work better if the communication partner tries to make statements. For example, instead of asking, “Are you looking at ‘e’?”, they could try stating, “You are looking at ‘e’.” The individual can still respond to the statement and make it clear that the communication partner is wrong, but the use of a statement stops them from feeling that the communication partner is ‘double checking’ or questioning their every utterance.

A pen and paper can be an incredibly useful piece of equipment for a struggling (or indeed any) communication partner! When engaged in a long conversation using an eye pointing alphabet chart, it is easy get a bit lost, or to forget where they have got to within a word. If letters are written down as they are spelled out, this can really help to keep the conversation on track. If pen and paper isn’t available, the note facility on a smartphone can work well.

Other strategies discussed in Chapter 11 like the communication partner attempting to predict (if the individual is happy with this), and the individual possibly using shorter sentences or simplified spelling can all help when communicating using eye pointing. Messages or phrases that are frequently communicated may also be able to be delivered more efficiently – see Chapter 10 for more information.
