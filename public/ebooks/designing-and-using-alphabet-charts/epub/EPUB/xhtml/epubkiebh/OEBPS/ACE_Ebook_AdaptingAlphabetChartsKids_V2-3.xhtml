Foreword

The aim of this guide is to help you design and use alphabet charts with people who can’t rely on speech to communicate.

Communicating a message involves more than just speech. We use many strategies to convey meaning – vocalisation, body language, gesture, facial expression, and so on. The tools and strategies in this guide are therefore only ever going to be one part of the way someone communicates.

However it is accomplished, communication is essential to life. It enables self expression, learning, having fun and being an active member of society.

This guide considers a large variety of paper-based tools and strategies available that can meet different needs at different times. These complement the wide range of electronic devices (sometimes known as speech output devices or voice output communication aids) that may be provided through the NHS or are available to purchase. Both these types of communication tools are known as Augmentative and Alternative Communication or AAC.

This guide is not suggesting that paper-based tools and strategies are in some way better than electronic ones. It is simply highlighting their existence and valuing the role they can play in communication. You may well find that many of the ideas and strategies are useful when it comes to other forms of communication such as electronic devices too.

Working with a Speech and Language Therapist is invaluable, as they can tailor the information and support within this guide to meet personal needs.
