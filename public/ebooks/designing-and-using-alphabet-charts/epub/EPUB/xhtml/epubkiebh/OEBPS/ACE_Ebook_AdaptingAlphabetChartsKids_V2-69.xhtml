Communication is a two way process and requires skills from both parties for it to be successful. This is true however the communication is taking place. However, when an alphabet chart or communication book are involved, there are a few additional considerations that can make a real difference to the success of the interaction.

A communication partner is anyone with whom the person who uses an alphabet chart communicates, and their role in the conversation and the skills that they bring can have a significant impact. The next section explores some key communication partner skills. There are also some tips for communication partners in the chapters on eye gaze and listener mediated scanning that specifically relate to those access methods.

﻿Communication Partner Skills

Understand How ‘Yes’ and ‘No’ are CommunicatedThis sounds so simple, but if a communication partner isn’t clear on this, then everything else is going to get very confusing very fast! If a personalised way of communicating ‘yes’ and ‘no’ like blinking or making a specific movement is used, the individual may want to make a point of demonstrating this to new communication partners.

Allow Time and Don’t RushAll will need to accept that communicating using the kind of tools and strategies discussed in this guide is going to take a little more time than using speech. A communication partner will need to give the person using an alphabet chart some additional time to construct their message.

Conversation can quickly become muddled if a communication partner rushes in with a second or even third question or observation, before the person has had a chance to finish responding to the first one.
