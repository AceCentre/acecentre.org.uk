ï»¿Tips for Communication Partners When Using Listener Mediated Scanning

See Chapter 11 for tips to help communication partners more generally.

The joy of paper-based listener mediated scanning approaches rather than software systems is that people tend to be much more responsive than computers! If the communication partner is offering letters on a chart and there is a sudden noisy distraction, they are able to pause and give time for that to pass. If the individual is tired or struggling to concentrate, the communication partner can adapt their speed accordingly. They may also be able to spot a âyesâ response that is being initiated, even if it is not quite on target due to fatigue or illness.

Although the strength of a communication partner is their flexibility, it is still essential to ensure that everyone is using the system consistently. If one communication partner groups the letters in a different way, for example, it can cause real confusion. Instructions are essential, and a short video may also be useful for new communication partners. Instructions can be kept in a Communication Passport and / or recorded on the chart itself.

Pen and paper (or similar) can really help the communication partner keep track of what is being communicated, You saw Katherine making use of this in the video of Katherine and Michael getting going with listener mediated scanning.

If listener mediated scanning is being done with a chart, one trick is to stick a self-adhesive dry erase sheet onto the back of the chart as shown in the video by John Costello.

When the communication partner is speaking aloud and / or pointing to letters, they need to ensure they are giving the individual enough time to respond. Communication partners should try and check that the individual is happy with the speed of their delivery.
