To deliver the alphabet, the communication partner reads aloud and / or points to the first item on each row in turn. If the target letter is ‘G’, for example, the individual would indicate ‘yes’ when the communication partner offered the vowel at the beginning of the row of letters containing the letter ‘G’ – i.e. ‘E’. The communication partner would then offer each letter in that row until the individual indicated ‘yes’ i.e. ‘E, F, G’ – ‘yes’. This technique is demonstrated in the next video:

There isn’t a ‘correct’ way of dividing up the alphabet into groups - using vowels is just one approach.

﻿﻿Using a Frequency Layout with Listener Mediated ScanningAlphabet charts arranged in alphabetical order can help both the individual and their communication partner relax, as both will be familiar with the ordering. However, it is possible to present letters in a more efficient manner by prioritising them according to their frequency of use within the English language. The letter ‘T’ appears in lots of words for example, but even with vowel-based grouping to speed things up, there is quite a lot of alphabet to get through before you reach it.

The alphabet chart below is grouped by rows and organised to ensure that letters that are used more frequently in the English language (like the letter ‘T’) are in top left area of the chart and towards the beginning of rows. Letters used less frequently (like the letter ‘Z’) are towards the bottom of the right area of the chart. This results in letters that are used more often being quicker to select than letters that are used less often.
