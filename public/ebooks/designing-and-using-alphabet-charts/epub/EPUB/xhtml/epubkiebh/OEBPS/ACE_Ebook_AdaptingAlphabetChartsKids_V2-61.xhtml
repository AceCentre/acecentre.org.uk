The alphabet is accessed using encoding. The young man first looked at the group of six letters and then clarified his choice by looking at the corresponding coloured border. However, the key words are accessed using a code. He first looked at the rectangle containing his target word and then communicated the numerical code associated with that word.

While this chart was developed for use with eye pointing, the approach could be adapted for someone using another access method.

Communication Charts

A communication chart is simply a page of useful words or phrases that can be pointed to or selected in some other way.

Some people find it helpful to have different charts for different locations. For example, a set of useful phrases that relate to washing and hygiene could be kept in the bathroom, and a set relating to the television kept in the living room.

Communication charts can take many different forms. The picture below shows a simple personalised chart that Pauline developed for her husband Dave to use whilst in hospital, alongside his alphabet chart. The list was pinned to the wall near his bed. Staff were trained to read aloud the list, and Dave raised his thumb when they reached the correct option.

Rather than having separate tools, you could have an alphabet chart on one side and a communication chart on the back. If the individual is not able to turn the chart over without assistance, make sure that they have a message on both sides that directs the communication partner to turn over the chart for them.
