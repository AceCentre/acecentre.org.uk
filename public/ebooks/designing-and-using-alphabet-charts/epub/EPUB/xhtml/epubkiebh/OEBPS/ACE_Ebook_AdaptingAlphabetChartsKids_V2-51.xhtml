The alphabet can also be encoded simply by position, without any additional visual cues like colour, shape or number. Having selected a group, the individual just indicates the location of the letter within the group. This is the approach used by the musician Jason Becker who has uses his Vocal Eyes chart to support his communication. He accesses this chart using eye movement and identifies the letter within a selected group of four by looking either up, down, left or right. You can find out more about the approach, see it being used and download a copy of the chart at jasonbeckerarchive.com/eye_communication.html.

In Summary - Encoded Layouts

Why consider an encoded layout?

Encoded layouts are designed to make it easier to select letters by reducing the number of target areas the individual selects from.

What access methods does it suit?

Encoded layouts are often used by people who use eye pointing to select letters. Encoded layouts can be used to help people to point to the alphabet, including by those using head movement.

What are the disadvantages?

The indvidual must make two movements to select one letter.

Encoding can feel a bit confusing initially, both for the individual and their communication partner(s). See Getting Started with Eye Pointing and Encoding for more information about strategies that can support. While focussed around eye pointing, these could be adapted for other access methods.
