If there are messages or phrases that someone is repeatedly spelling out, or would be useful for them to deliver quickly, it may be helpful to collect them together in a communication chart or book, or place them on the alphabet chart itself. Alternatively, you could identify a letter code for important phrases.

As well as aiding with the efficiency of communication, giving access to words and phrases can help if someone is tired or unwell and wants an ‘easier’ way of communicating. They can also help someone to join in fast moving conversations as whole phrases can be delivered quickly. For example, phrases like ‘That’s what I think too’, ‘I’m not sure about that’ or ‘you can’t be serious’, all help to keep conversation flowing.

When thinking about messages or phrases, try and ensure they are personalised to the individual and phrased in a way that they would choose to say them. Ideally the individual themselves would be involved in identifying these.

We all use language in different ways in different contexts, and someone may need different types of phrases for the various facets of their life. Language use with peers may be quite different from language use with professionals, for example. Language use can also be affected by factors such as culture, age, etc. If you do download a template for a communication chart or book (see Chapter 13 for more information about resources available), it really is worth taking the time to personalise any suggested phrases.

If someone requires access to messages or phrases in more than one language, it is possible to simply translate messages identified into the relevant language(s). However, it may be necessary or desirable to have some additional phrases that are specific to the relevant language or culture. If the individual can’t assist, working with a translator or family member is invaluable as online translators may not be accurate enough for this purpose.
