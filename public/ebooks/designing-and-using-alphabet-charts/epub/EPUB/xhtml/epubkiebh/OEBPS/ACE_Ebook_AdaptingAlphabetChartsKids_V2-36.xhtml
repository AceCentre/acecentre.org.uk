Using Auditory Scanning

 

﻿Making a Selection when using Listener Mediated Scanning

As discussed above, all that is required to use listener mediated scanning is a single signal that indicates ‘yes, that’s the one I want’. There is no real need to say ‘no, not that one’ to options that are not wanted, as the communication partner can just assume it is a ‘no’, unless the individual responds ‘yes’. However, as always, it is down to personal preference.

Using only a ‘yes’ signal makes things much quicker as the communication partner doesn’t have to wait for a response after each letter. In addition, the individual can relax until the desired option is seen and / or heard which can be less tiring than having to communicate ‘no’ repeatedly.

The downside of this approach is that if the communication partner misses the individual’s ‘yes’ signal, they will have to wait for the communication partner to go through the alphabet, or section of the alphabet, a second time. Such problems tend to arise with communication partners who are less familiar with the strategy, who may rush through the options or not look out for the signal. Enskilling communication partners by giving opportunities for practise can really help.

When someone is getting started with listener mediated scanning, it may help them to use both the ‘yes’ and ‘no’ responses. In the video in Chapter 1, Alli waits for Ethan to indicate ‘no, not that one’ before moving onto the next letter.

Similarly, if a communication partner is really struggling, an individual might choose to use the second signal that indicates ‘no, not that one’. This means that after each letter is offered, the communication partner must wait for either a ‘yes’ or a ‘no’ signal from the individual before offering the next letter. It will slow things down but might just help someone who is prone to rushing or forgetting to look out for the individual’s signal. Hopefully as the communication partner gains confidence, the individual can revert to using the single ‘yes’ signal if they prefer.

It can help to write the instructions on how the communication partner should use the chart on the chart itself. Make sure the signals used to communicate ‘yes’ and ‘no’ (if relevant) are also documented.

Getting Started with Listener Mediated Scanning and the Alphabet

If someone has no experience of uslng listener mediated scanning, you may want to introduce the whole concept away from the alphabet. You could use listener mediated scanning with pictures of pop stars or whatever appealed to the individual. Alternatively, you could keep with the alphabet, but begin by selecting letters within a game, or use the chart in a fun conversation where misspelled words are seen as entertaining. This helps to lift the pressure and builds confidence in using the chart.

As discussed above, when getting started you may want to seek a response to each option rather than relying upon ‘yes, that’s the one I want’.

Another way of getting started with listener mediated scanning the alphabet is to﻿ ﻿simplify the presentation of the alphabet itself. This is the approach that was used with Ethan. Rather than showing Ethan the full alphabet, Alli presented him with one row of letters at a time, using a chart similar to the one illustrated next.

This reduced the cognitive load, as there were less letters to look at. It also simplified the scan process, as Ethan just had to say whether he could see his target letter or not. If he couldn’t see the letter, Alli would turn the page and ask if it was now visible. Once they were on the correct page, Alli could then offer each letter in turn.
