Black and yellow are not the only high contrast colour combinations, and it may be that a different colour scheme works better for an individual.

The choice of font size may be affected by visual ability. If someone is long sighted, you may want to consider whether they require their glasses to see the alphabet chart. If the person does not always have their glasses to hand or there are times when they are not worn, you may want to make a chart where the letters are large enough to be seen without having to rely upon glasses. You may also want to take the visual skills of any key communication partners into account, as they need to be able to easily see what is being pointed to.

To help with clarity, you may want to consider how much space there is between each letter on the alphabet chart. Increasing the amount of space between each letter may make it clearer to the communication partner exactly which letter is being pointed to. As discussed in Chapter 2, a keyguard may also help here. Similarly, if a chart is being used with listener mediated scanning, then increasing the amount of space between each letter may help the individual to see what the communication partner is pointing to. However, too much space can make the chart slower to use.

More Significant Visual Difficulties

Sometimes complex visual issues require additional modifications, and some ideas are shared below. If visual access to a chart proves too difficult, or is difficult at certain times, you might want to consider introducing a purely auditory listener mediated scan to access the alphabet. In other words, the communication partner speaks aloud the alphabet and the individual responds when they hear their target letter.

﻿Simplifying the PresentationSome visual issues may affect eye movement itself, making it difficult for someone to visually scan the whole alphabet. Sometimes adapting how the alphabet is presented can help. One option is to reduce the number of letters that are presented at one time, perhaps presenting them in a series of horizontal or vertical strips.

The alphabet chart below was set up for someone with Progressive Supranuclear Palsy (PSP). They were able to make horizontal movements with their eyes but found looking up or down difficult. High contrast colours were also helpful for them. The alphabet is presented in a series of horizontal strips with ‘A to D’ on the first page, ‘E to H’ on the second, ‘I to N’ on the third, ‘O to T’ on the fourth and ‘U to Z’ on the final page. Like with the simplified listener mediated scanning chart above, an additional page of commands like ‘space’ and ‘delete’ could be incorporated.

The chart was accessed using listener mediated scanning. If the page contained the target letter, they would indicate ‘yes’, and each letter on that page would be offered in turn until a selection was made. Otherwise the communication partner would turn the page and offer the next set of letters.
