A printed QWERTY alphabet chart does feel different to use than a keyboard however, so it may still be worth giving an individual who is an experienced QWERTY user a chance to explore other layouts before making a decision, as in the choosing an alphabet chart video of Bridget and Sara above. A computer keyboard is often used with ten fingers. If someone is pointing to a printed chart with one finger or using a pointing tool, it will feel different.

QWERTY layouts can work well for people accessing charts by pointing, but for those using alternative access methods such as eye pointing and listener mediated scanning, it may be worth considering a different layout.

The history of the QWERTY layout is worth bearing in mind too. It was devised in the late 1800s for use on a manual typewriter to reduce the jams that could occur when typing neighbouring letters consecutively. So the layout was designed to meet a need that no longer exists, and the layout is not optimised for speed and efficiency on a contemporary keyboard.

In Summary - QWERTY

Why consider QWERTY?

QWERTY is a keyboard layout that is familiar to people who have used computer keyboards, or may become familiar to a child who will also be using a standard computer keyboard.

What access methods does it suit?

QWERTY is best suited to access by pointing.

What are the disadvantages?

For someone who is not familiar with a computer keyboard, it may take extra time to learn where to find the letters. The QWERTY layout was not designed to be an efficient layout of letters.

Many people are familiar with a QWERTY layout as this is the arrangement of letters that you find on computer keyboards. For those who know the layout well, or who will be using a standard computer keyboard regularly, this may be a good option to explore. They will already know the location of the letters and consequently won’t have to hunt around for them, which slows down the communication process.
