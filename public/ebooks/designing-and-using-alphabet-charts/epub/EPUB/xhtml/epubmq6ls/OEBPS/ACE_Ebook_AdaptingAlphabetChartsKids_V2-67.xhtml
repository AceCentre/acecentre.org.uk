confusion as so many words start with ‘TH’. One way round this is to include a special character or symbol on the alphabet chart such as the * character that signals that the next selection is a letter code. To communicate ‘I’m thirsty’, the person would select ‘*TH’.

Using letter codes can really help to speed up communication with familiar communication partners and are also useful if tiredness is an issue. One gentleman shared that he liked using them because you can say so much with just two movements.

Letter codes are slightly tricker when communicating with less familiar communication partners. However, they can still be used provided there is a clearly displayed chart that enables them to ‘look up’ the code.

﻿Using Words and Phrases as Topic Cues

Until now, this chapter has focussed on ways in which words and messages can be delivered more efficiently within conversation. However, there is another way of using words and phrases alongside an alphabet chart.

Rather than using words and phrases as pre-identified messages, instead, they can be used as a cue to the communication partner as to the topic of conversation. Topic cues can be useful when dealing with a change of subject or if the person is initiating communication, and they can help resolve communication breakdown. They tend to be most useful for people who use some speech to communicate, but have times when communication partners cannot understand what they are saying.

A list of the types of things that are regularly talked about could be made and then used as a standalone communication tool, printed on the back of an alphabet chart or even printed on the alphabet chart itself. The topics chosen will be personal to the individual and their environment.
