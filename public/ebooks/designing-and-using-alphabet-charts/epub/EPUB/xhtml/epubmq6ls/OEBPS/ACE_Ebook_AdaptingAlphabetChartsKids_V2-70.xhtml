It can feel strange at first, but it does really help if the communication partner can relax and wait calmly for the person using the alphabet chart to say what they need to say.

Learn How the Communication System WorksOne of the most helpful things a communication partner can do is to take the time to fully understand how the system being used works. This is where a communication passport can really come into its own.

Sometimes it is helpful for a communication partner to be given time to practise the system away from the pressure of an important conversation. The person using the alphabet chart might begin by spelling out an agreed word and then progress to the communication partner trying to find out something fun like the name of their first pet, for example. A new communication partner might also find it helpful to try using the tool themselves to spell out words.

It’s not just the mechanics of how letters are selected that the communication partner needs to learn. They need to understand rules like how the person will indicate that they have something to say in the first place, how they will signal a space, or how they will let the communication partner know that something’s gone wrong. These rules are often the difference between conversation flowing and communication breakdown.

Keep Track of ConversationsTrying to remember where they’ve got to when communicating with someone who is spelling letter-by-letter can be stressful for a communication partner and can result in them losing the thread of the conversation.

Encouraging a communication partner to write down each letter as it is selected it can really lift the pressure - you can see Katherine doing this in the video below. Making sure there is pen and paper to hand can help. Alternatively, a whiteboard with dry erase pen or LCD writing tablet can work well. If there is no pen or paper to hand, people often have a smartphone on them and could be encouraged to use its note taking app.

Predicting What is Being Communicated (But only if the person allows it!) If the context is known, a familiar communication partner may be able to predict a word or even sentence being communicated after only a couple of letters. In the video in Chapter 1, having established that Anjali had been learning about elephants, Suzanne asked which type of elephant had the biggest ears - Anjali pointed to the letter ‘I’ and Suzanne confidently predicted ‘Indian’.

The person using an alphabet chart could be encouraged to reflect on how they feel about people predicting. Some regard it as beneficial as it can speed up communication, while others find it irritating. It is also worth noting that some communication partners are better at it than others! There is no right or wrong approach. If someone does feel strongly either way, this could be documented alongside any other instructions or even written on the tool itself if it is a blanket rule.
