Eye Pointing to Letters ﻿﻿Using Encoding Rather than requiring the communication partner to work out which of twenty-six individual letters are being looked at, most eye pointing charts make use of encoding. Encoding groups letters together, reducing the number of target areas that the communication partner must differentiate between. This makes it much easier for the communication partner to follow the eye movements. However, it does mean that two eye movements are required per letter selected. You can read more about this approach to arranging letters here.

In this video, you see Tiago selecting the letters ‘b’, ‘a’ and ‘r’ on his encoded eye pointing alphabet chart. The video is filmed from the perspective of the communication partner, so shows the mirror image of the letter layout that Tiago is looking at.

Eye pointing to letters on an encoded eye pointing chart

In the video, you see that to select ‘b’ Tiago first eye points to the group (referred to as a block in the video) containing the letters ‘a, b, c, d, e, f’ and then eye points to the black dot. To select ‘a’ he again eye points to the group (or block) containing ‘a, b, c, d, e, f’, but this time eye points to the red dot. Finally, to select ‘r’ he eye points to the group containing ‘m, n, o, p, q, r’ and then eye points to the pink dot.

Encoding means that instead of needing to point with his eyes to twenty-six different areas, Tiago only needs to point to six.

The Frenchay E-tran frame is a well known option for eye pointing and, like Tiago’s eye pointing chart, makes use of encoding. The letter layout and colours used differ slightly to that used by Tiago, but the approach is similar.

The Frenchay E-Tran Frame

To select the letter ‘B’ on the Frenchay E-Tran Frame, the individual would first eye point to the group that contains the characters ‘A, B, C, D, E, F’. To clarify that ‘B’ was the desired letter (rather than ‘A, C, D, E or F’), they would then eye point to the yellow dot, because ‘B’ is coloured yellow.
