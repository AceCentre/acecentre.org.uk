Simple changes to the chart itself can also make a difference. You could think about adjusting the size of the chart, the font size and /or the amount of space between the letters.

Other things to consider are:

• thinking about a chart’s position, including placing it at an angle rather than flat on a surface

• fixing a chart in place

• using a pointing tool and / or using a chart with a built in keyguard

If these tweaks don’t resolve the problem, it may be worth thinking about using an encoded chart or a coded chart, as discussed in Chapter 8. Alternatively, you might want to consider some of the other approaches discussed in this guide such as eye pointing or listener mediated scanning.

Positioning Alphabet ChartsIt is worth thinking about where the chart is positioned, as small changes can sometimes have a big impact on useabliity. The chart needs to be somewhere that is easy and comfortable to use and see. If someone is using their right hand for example, simply moving the chart slightly to the right might make it easier to use.

﻿Alphabet charts are often placed flat on a table or tray. However, for some, angling the chart by placing it on some sort of wedge, a writing slope or even an adjustable document stand can make it easier to use. Typically, a gentle slope is all that’s needed. Writing slopes are available from a range of mainstream suppliers as they are used in both offices and schools, but slopes can also be made using just a tea towel / similar and some Velcro™.
