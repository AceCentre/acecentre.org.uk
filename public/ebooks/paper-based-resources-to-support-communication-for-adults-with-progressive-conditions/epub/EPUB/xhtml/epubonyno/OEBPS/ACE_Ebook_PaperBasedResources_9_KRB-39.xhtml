How it Works

The simplest way of scanning through the alphabet is for the communication partner to offer each letter in turn in alphabetical order as in the video above. This is how Dave and Pauline choose to use their alphabet chart. Pauline holds the chart so Dave can see it and points to each letter in turn until Dave puts up his thumb. Pauline then speaks this letter aloud and provided Dave doesn’t indicate she has made a mistake, she writes this down and then starts again at the beginning of the alphabet. It is a system that works well for them both.

While straightforward, pointing to each letter in turn is not necessarily quickest way of delivering the alphabet. To select the letter ‘T’ for example, you must first wait while your communication partner points to and / or speaks aloud the nineteen letters that precede it.

To speed up the process, the letters can be grouped together. The communication partner first scans through the groups. You choose the group containing your letter, then they offer each letter within that group.

This letters in this ABC alphabet chart have been grouped in rows. Each row begins with a vowel. This breaks the alphabet up into five groups that are identified by the name of the vowel at the start of each group / row. This chart, and others like it, are available from the Ace Centre website acecentre.org.uk.

Chapter 7 - Selecting letters by looking and / or listening and giving a simple response

As demonstrated in the next video, the communication partner reads aloud and / or points to the first item on each row in turn. If your target letter is ‘G’, for example, you would indicate ‘yes’ when they offer the vowel at the beginning of the row of letters containing the letter ‘G’ – i.e. ‘E’. The communication partner would then offer each letter in that row until you indicate ‘yes’ i.e. ‘E, F, G’ – ‘yes’.
