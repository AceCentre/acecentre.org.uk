While an E-tran frame (or similar) is most commonly used for eye pointing, there are alternatives. You could eye point to a chart presented on a recipe book / document stand or easel file or make use of a whiteboard like Beatrice. Such tools need to be positioned with care to ensure the communication partner can still easily follow your eye movement.

EncodingRather than requiring the communication partner to work out which of twenty-six individual letters you are looking at, most E-tran frames make use of encoding. Note encoding can also support access to alphabet charts for people using hand movement.

In this video, you see Rosemary selecting the letters ‘W’ and ‘I’ on the encoded Frenchay E-tran frame. The video is filmed from the perspective of the communication partner, so we are seeing the mirror image of what

Rosemary is looking at. To select ‘W’ she first eye points to the group containing ‘S, T, U, V, W, X’ and then eye points to the black dot. To select ‘I’ she first eye points to the group containing ‘G, H, I, J, K, L’ and then eye points to the blue dot. This is the first time she has used the frame, but having tried it out, she felt that it would be useful to have as a back up to her other electronic communication aids.

Encoding groups letters together, reducing the number of target areas that the communication partner must differentiate between. Instead of looking at an individual letter, with encoding you first point with your eyes to the group that contains your desired letter. You then look a second time to clarify which of the letters in the group is your target. Encoding means you need to point to far fewer areas with your eyes (on the Frenchay frame in the video, Rosemary only needs to eye points to six areas to communicate the full alphabet). This makes it much easier for the communication partner to read your eye movements. However, it does mean that two eye movements are required per letter selected.
