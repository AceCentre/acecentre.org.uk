Foreword

This guide provides information about paper-based tools and strategies that can support communication if speech is becoming or has become difficult due to an acquired progressive condition. It is written for people living with such a condition and also for family, carers and relevant professionals.

Communicating a message involves more than just speech. We use many strategies to convey meaning – vocalisation, body language, gesture, facial expression, and so on. The tools and strategies in this guide are therefore only ever going to be one part of the way you communicate.

All the strategies within the guide depend upon you being able to communicate ‘yes’ and, ideally, ‘no’ in some way. If you are communicating these in a way that someone might not immediately recognise (blinking perhaps or looking in a certain direction), it is worth making sure that everyone knows how you do this and are responding to the same thing.

As you will see in this guide, there are a large variety of paper-based tools and strategies available that can meet different needs at different times. These complement the wide range of electronic devices (sometimes known as speech output devices or voice output communication aids) that may be provided through the NHS or are available to purchase. You may sometimes hear both these types of communication tools referred to as Augmentative and Alternative Communication or AAC.

This guide is not suggesting that paper-based tools and strategies are in some way better than electronic ones. It is simply highlighting their existence and valuing the role they can play in communication. Some people may choose to use paper-based tools and strategies as a backup for when an electronic aid is unavailable. Some may use them interchangeably with an electronic aid, while others may find them more effective than

an electronic aid, or simply choose not to make use of an electronic aid at all. It is all about personal choice and finding out what best meets your needs at different moments in time. You may well want to pull together a range of tools that are flexible enough to enable you to vary how you communicate depending upon mood, energy levels, well-being, who you are communicating with or other factors.

If you need to communicate in more than one language, you may require separate resources for each language. Alternatively, depending upon the language concerned, you may be able to meet the needs of both using the same communication tool(s).

Within this guide, you will be introduced to a number of people with progressive conditions who have kindly agreed to share their stories. You will see that they choose to use paper-based tools and strategies in very different ways.

One such person is Beatrice, who has had a diagnosis of Motor Neuron Disease for some years and is not able to use speech to communicate. She now uses eye pointing to access a bespoke whiteboard that was developed by one of her carers. Beatrice also has a tablet computer that she controls by eye pointing, and uses for all sorts of activities including speech output. However, if the computer is not on and in position, Beatrice chooses first to use her whiteboard if she has something to communicate. It’s also her first choice if she is tired. Janet, one of her main carers, describes the whiteboard as “Beatrice’s lifeline”. On using the whiteboard, Beatrice reflects that for her, “it’s quicker when you are halfway through dinner and decide you want more salt.” You can read more about how Beatrice communicates here.
