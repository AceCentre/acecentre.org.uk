Supporting the use of an Alphabet Chart if Pointing Accurately to Letters is a Little Difficult

A simple way of helping communication partners to follow what you are communicating is to provide instructions or a bit of training on how you use the chart. For example, you might print along the top of the chart “I use my index finger to select letters - ignore my other fingers”. This can help a communication partner know what to focus on. You could also ask your communication partner to speak aloud or write down the letters as you point to them, so you can both confirm that you are on the right track. As discussed above, it is quickest if you only let the communication partner know when they have got it wrong, rather than agreeing that they have got it right with each letter selected.

Simple changes to the chart itself can also make a difference. A good place to start is to think about adjusting the size of the chart, the font size and /or the amount of space between the letters.

You could also look at:

• Fixing the chart in place and thinking about its position, including at an angle rather than flat on a surface

• Using a pointing tool, and / or

• Using a chart with a built in keyguard

If these tweaks don’t resolve the problem, it may be worth thinking about using an encoded chart or a coded chart, or considering some of the other communication tools discussed in this guide that don’t require you to point directly to letters.

Fixing the Chart in Place and Thinking About its PositionIf a chart is slipping around on a tray or table or even a knee, this can make it more difficult to use. Sometimes fixing it in place on a firm surface with a sticky material like Blu Tack or Velcro™ can make an enormous difference to being able to point clearly to letters. Placing non-slip materials like Dycem®, Tenura or similar underneath a chart can help too.

It is worth thinking about where the chart is positioned. It needs to be somewhere that is easy and comfortable for you to use. If you are using your right hand for example, rather than having the chart centrally on a tray in front of you, simply moving the chart to the right might make it easier to use.

Alphabet charts are often placed flat on a table or tray. However, for some, angling the chart by placing it on some sort of wedge, a writing slope or even an adjustable document stand can make it easier to use. Typically, a gentle slope is all that’s needed. Writing slopes are available from a range of mainstream suppliers as they are used in both offices and schools, but you could also make your own using just a tea towel and some Velcro™.
