Just like alphabet charts, symbol-based resources can be accessed by pointing with hands, head, eyes, listener mediated scanning or some combination of these methods.

Specialist resource making software can be used to make symbol-based charts and books. A Speech and Language Therapist can support the development of personalised symbol-based communication charts and books, and they may well have access to such software. You can also contact Ace Centre for more information - acecentre.org.uk or freephone 0800 080 3115.

An Example of Bringing Symbols into a Communication System

Ace Centre supported a gentleman whom we will call Harold. He had a diagnosis of atypical MND and experienced some cognitive decline. This meant that spelling became increasingly difficult for him.

He initially made use of an alphabet chart with listener mediated scanning like those discussed in Chapter 7, to support his communication. He found it difficult to remember how to spell full words, but for a time, he could manage the first few letters of a word. This was often enough for him to get his message across. For example, to ask to look at his train magazine, he might select the letters ‘T, R’. Those that knew him well were then able to successfully guess what he wanted.

However, as Harold’s condition progressed, spelling became more difficult. He came to rely more and more on a communication book to support him. The book contained many phrases, so these were categorised. Harold was involved in developing this book, particularly choosing what messages he wanted in the Chat category, including ‘I love you’ and ‘Oh bother!’.

The communication book was accessed using listener mediated scanning, which meant that Harold didn’t need to read the messages in the book, he just had to listen to a communication partner offering a number of options and indicate ‘yes’ when he heard the message that he wanted to communicate.

To help him further, some of the messages had symbols alongside them which the communicate partner would point to as they spoke the message. For example, the phrase ‘I love you” was represented by a symbol of a heart, and ‘Oh bother!’ was represented by a symbol of a person holding their hand over their mouth in a concerned way.
