This alphabet chart was made for someone with PSP (Progressive Supranuclear Palsy). They were able to make horizontal movements with their eyes but found looking up or down difficult. The alphabet is presented in alphabetical order: ‘A to D’ is on the first page, ‘E to H’ is on the second, ‘I to N’ is on the third, ‘O to T’ is on the fourth and ‘U to Z’ is on the final page. The chart was accessed using listener mediated scanning. If the page contained the target letter, they would indicate ‘yes’, and each letter on that page would be offered in turn until a selection was made. Otherwise the communication partner would turn the page and offer the next set of letters. ‘Space’ and ‘mistake’ could easily be added to this chart to help conversation.

The positioning of a chart may also help visual access. Placing a chart on a wedge or document stand, for example, might help you to see it better. If you have a wheelchair (or other) mounting system, it might also be possible to attach your alphabet chart to this. This should be discussed with the suppliers of the mounting system.

A visual field issue (where there is effectively a blind spot or area in your vision) might mean that the positioning of the alphabet chart affects how easy it is to see it.

If visual access to a chart proves too difficult, or is difficult at certain times, you might want to consider using a purely auditory listener mediated scan to access the alphabet. In other words, the communication partner speaks aloud the alphabet and you indicate when you hear your target letter.

Tactile AlphabetIt is possible to produce an alphabet chart in braille if required. Braille keyboard stickers are available from the RNIB shop. Alternatively, you could produce braille letters using the 6dot™ Braille Label Maker™ which is available from Logan Technologies.

When constructing an alphabet chart in braille, make sure the printed letter is included alongside the braille letter so that communication partners can understand what is being pointed to.

An alternative to braille is Moon. Moon is a tactile alphabet that has sometimes been used by people who have lost sight later in life or whose lack of sensitivity means they struggle to perceive the difference between the braille letters. See the RNIB website here for more information. Note that the RNIB does not actively support Moon and has concerns about its viability.
