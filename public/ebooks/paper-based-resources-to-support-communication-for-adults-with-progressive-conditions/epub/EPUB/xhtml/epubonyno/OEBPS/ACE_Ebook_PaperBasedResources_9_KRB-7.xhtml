Introduction

This guide will take you through a range of paper-based tools and strategies that can be used to support communication if speech is difficult. For support with this, and for more information about electronic devices, you may want to consult your Speech and Language therapist and / or a specialist centre such as Ace Centre.

Chapter 1 introduces you to a number of people with acquired conditions who have generously shared how they are using paper-based systems to support their communication. In this guide, the people with whom you are communicating are referred to as communication partners. Communication by its very nature involves two or more people and therefore your communication partners are key to the whole process.

Chapter 2 takes a look at how communication partners can help and considers what advice you may want to give them. The next few sections take a detailed look at the tools, techniques and strategies that can be used to support communication.

Chapter 3 looks at using writing.

Chapter 4 explores pointing to letters on an alphabet chart.

Chapter 5 briefly considers using head movement to access an alphabet chart.

Chapter 6 looks in detail at using eye pointing to select letters.

Chapter 7 is all about listener mediated scanning, which is where your communication partner points to and / or speaks aloud the alphabet and you indicate when they have reached your target letter.

Chapter 8 takes a quick look at options that combine some of the above.

Chapter 9 then looks at modifications that can be made to these tools to make them work better for you, however you use them.

Chapter 10 looks at how an alphabet chart can be supplemented with a collection of words and phrases. These can be presented on a chart or in a book, and / or accessed via letter codes (where you allocate an abbreviation to a phrase - rather like fyi is an abbreviation of ‘for your information’).

Chapter 11 considers how communication can be supported if you find spelling is or is becoming difficult.

Chapter 12 takes a practical look at how you can source or make the communication tools discussed in this guide.

Finally, Chapter 13 suggests ways of accessing further information, and provides links to websites dedicated to specific conditions that can impact upon speech such as Parkinson’s Disease or Motor Neuron Disease.
