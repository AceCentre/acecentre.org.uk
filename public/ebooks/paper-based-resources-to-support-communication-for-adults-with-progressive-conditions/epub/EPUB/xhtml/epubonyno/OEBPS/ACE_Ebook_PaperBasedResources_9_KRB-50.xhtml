Size of the Chart, Font Type and Size and the Amount of Space Between the Letters

Alphabet charts are often presented on A4 paper. However, there is no reason why other sizes shouldnâ€™t be used. Bridget found a smaller chart easier to use, while others may prefer a larger chart. For charts accessed by listener mediated scanning or eye pointing, it may be about finding the size that is easiest both to hold and see.

The choice of font is down to personal preference, but it is worth making sure that it is as easy to read as possible.

When producing a printed alphabet chart, people tend to use black font on a white background. However, there are times when other colour combinations can be useful or simply preferable. This chart, for example, may be easier to see for someone with certain visual difficulties because of the use of the high contrast colours black and yellow:

Black and yellow are not the only high contrast colour options, and it may be that a different combination works better for you.

The choice of font size may be affected by visual ability. If you wear glasses or contact lenses, you may want to think about whether you need to be wearing these to see the alphabet chart. If you do not always have your glasses to hand or there are times when you do not wear them, you may want to make a chart where the letters are large enough to be seen without having to rely upon using glasses or contact lenses. You may also want to take the visual skills of any key communication partners into account, as they need to be able to easily see what is being pointed to.

To help with clarity, you may want to think about how much space there is between each letter on the alphabet chart. Increasing the amount of space between each letter may make it clearer to the communication partner exactly which letter you are pointing to. Similarly, if a chart is being used with listener mediated scanning, then increasing the amount of space between each letter may help you to see what the communication partner is pointing to. However, too much space can make the chart slower to use.

Special Considerations for more Significant Visual Difficulties

Sometimes visual issues are more complex and simply changing the font size or colour scheme is not quite enough.

Eye Movement LimitationsWith some acquired conditions, eye movement itself can become difficult, which may make it hard to visually scan the whole alphabet. Sometimes adapting how the alphabet is presented can help. One option is to reduce the number of letters that are presented at one time, perhaps presenting them in a horizontal or vertical strip.
