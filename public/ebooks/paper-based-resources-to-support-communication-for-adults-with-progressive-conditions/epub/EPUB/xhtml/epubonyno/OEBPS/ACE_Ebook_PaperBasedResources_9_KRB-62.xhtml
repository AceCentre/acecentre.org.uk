Sometimes people are concerned that their spelling isn’t very good. However, it doesn’t need to be perfect to get your message across successfully using an alphabet chart.

In fact, like Lorraine finds, using simplified phonetic spellings like ‘wen’ for ‘when’ or ‘u’ for ‘you’ can in fact be a useful strategy to speed things along.

In some cases, just being able to identify the first letter or two of a word can be enough to help cue a familiar communication partner into what you are trying to communicate.

However, if spelling is proving to be too difficult to support your communication effectively, a communication chart or book containing useful words or phrases will come into its own.

If reading words is difficult, then there are options that can be explored.

One solution is to use a listener mediated scanning communication book where the communication partner reads aloud the options as they point to them, or even just reads them aloud without showing you the options. You just need to listen to the messages and indicate when they reach the desired option.

Another alternative is create a communication chart or book where the words and phrases are associated with symbols and photographs.

Symbols differ from clipart in that they are drawn in a consistent way and can be used to illustrate a much wider range of vocabulary. There are several symbol languages in use in the UK, including, but not limited to, Picture Communication Symbols (PCS®), SymbolStix and Widgit symbols.

If an electronic communication aid with symbols is also being used, the same type of symbols should be used within the communication chart or book.
