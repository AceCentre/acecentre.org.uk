There are different frequency layouts available, and they will differ from one language to another. Jean-Dominique Bauby dictated his famous memoir The Diving Bell and the Butterfly using listener mediated scanning with an alphabet chart that was organised according to the frequency of use of letters in the French alphabet. He indicated ‘yes’ to a target letter by blinking.

Listener Mediated Scanning Without a Chart

For those with significant visual difficulties, there is no need to rely on a printed chart. The alphabet can simply be spoken aloud, with you indicating when the target letter is reached. Even when there are no visual issues, you and your communication partner may find you prefer to manage without a printed chart. This can also provide a way of communicating in the dark.

Listener mediated scanning without a chart is also known as auditory scanning.

Auditory scanning is demonstrated in the video below. In this example, the alphabet has been divided into two groups – ‘A to M’ and ‘N to Z’.

The communication partner first offers ‘A’ or ‘N’, then goes on to speak aloud the letters that follow either ‘A’ or ‘N’ depending upon the choice made. The alphabet could just as easily be divided up by vowels or in any other way that makes sense to you.

The video below, by Margaret Cotts, shows a gentleman with MND (referred to here as ALS) using auditory scanning. He is using a vowel-based layout, and the beginning of the video explains how this works. As the video progresses, you will see that both he and his communication partner are so familiar with the system that they can manage without the chart. They also make use of letter codes.
