Alphabet charts may be used alongside speech if there are issues with clarity. Sometimes simply pointing to the first letter of the word is enough to cue your communication partner into what you are saying. The video below by Margaret Cotts demonstrates this strategy:

Others use alphabet charts to spell out entire words and phrases.

Another strategy that can help to cue a communication partner into what you are saying is to tell them the topic of conversation. This can be especially helpful if you are changing the subject or initiating communication. You could produce a list of the types of things you regularly talk about and print this on the back of your alphabet chart, ready to point to when needed. The topics will be personal to you, but might include: TV, current affairs, reminiscing, the past, the future, chores, house maintenance issues, diary planning / events, family members, friends, other person, pets, finances, health concerns, specific hobbies, etc.

An example of a Topic Cueing Chart that can be used as a standalone communication tool or printed on the reverse of an alphabet chart

More information about letter and topic cueing can be found on the ALS Program area of the Boston Children’s Hospital website. They also have letter and topic cueing charts available for download.

To make an alphabet chart and / or a cueing chart into a robust communication tool, it is worth either laminating it or printing it on specialist paper. The laminate might also make it easier for you to slide your finger around on the chart.
