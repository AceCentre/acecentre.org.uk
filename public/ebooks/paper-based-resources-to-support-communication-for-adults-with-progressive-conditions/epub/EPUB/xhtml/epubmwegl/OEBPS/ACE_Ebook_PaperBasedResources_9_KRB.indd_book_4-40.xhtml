There isn’t a ‘correct’ way of dividing up the alphabet into groups - using vowels is just one approach.

Using a Frequency LayoutAlphabet charts arranged in alphabetical order can help both you and your communication partner relax, as both of you will have been familiar with the ordering since childhood.

However, it is possible to present letters in a more efficient manner by prioritising them according to their frequency of use within the English

language. The letter ‘T’ appears in lots of words for example, but even with vowel-based grouping to speed things up, there is quite a lot of alphabet to get through before you reach it.

The alphabet chart below is grouped by rows and organised to ensure that letters that are used more frequently in the English language (like the letter ‘T’) are in top left area of the chart and towards the beginning of rows. Letters used less frequently (like the letter ‘Z’) are towards the bottom of the right area of the chart. This results in letters that you use more often being quicker to select than letters that you use less often.

To use the chart, the communication partner offers the first letter in each row.

Once you indicate ‘yes’ to the row that contains your letter, they then offer each letter along that row until you make your selection. For example, to communicate the letter ‘S’, the communication partner would first offer ‘space’ then ‘T’ then ‘N’. Once they had offered ‘N’, you would indicate ‘yes’ as ‘N’ is at the start of the row that contains the letter ‘S’. They would then offer ‘N’ then ‘S’ and you would indicate ‘yes’.
