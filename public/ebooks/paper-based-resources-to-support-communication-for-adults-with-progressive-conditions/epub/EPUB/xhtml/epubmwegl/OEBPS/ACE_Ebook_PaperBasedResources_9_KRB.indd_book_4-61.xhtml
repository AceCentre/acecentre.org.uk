Letter Codes (Also Known as Abbreviation-Expansion)

Letter codes, also known as abbreviation–expansion, represent a longer word or phrase with a short abbreviation. Abbreviations are used in everyday language, particularly with the advent of the Internet and messaging, and you may well have come across LOL being used for ‘laugh out loud’ or FYI for ‘for your information.’ However, you may want to develop your own personal letter codes to meet your specific needs. For example, if you found yourself regularly spelling ‘Is there anything good on TV tonight’, you might decide to abbreviate this to something like GTV.

Some choose to use letter codes for just the odd phrase that they find themselves spelling a lot. However, others spend time developing a system of codes. A pet owner, for example, might choose to have a set of codes around animal care like OC for ‘Please let the cat out’, IC for ‘Please let the cat in’, FC for ‘Please feed the cat’ or perhaps LC for ‘leave the cat alone’.

Rosemary, who was featured in Chapter 6 using a Frenchay E-tran frame, spent some time during our visit exploring whether letter codes might be useful for her. She often asks for things to be passed to her, and we discussed perhaps having a set of codes beginning with the letter ‘G’ for such requests e.g. ‘GIP’ for ‘get my iPad’.

Letter codes work best if the abbreviations chosen don’t appear at the start of commonly used words. For example, ‘TH’ for ‘I’m thirsty’ could lead to confusion as so many words start with ‘TH’. One way round this is to include a special character or symbol on your alphabet chart that signals that you are going to deliver a letter code such as the * character. To communicate ‘I’m thirsty’, you could select ‘*TH’.

Using letter codes can really help to speed up communication with familiar communication partners and are also useful if tiredness is an issue. One gentleman shared that he liked using them because you can say so much with just two movements. Letter codes can also work with people who don’t know you well, provided there is a clearly displayed chart for them to use to interpret the code.
