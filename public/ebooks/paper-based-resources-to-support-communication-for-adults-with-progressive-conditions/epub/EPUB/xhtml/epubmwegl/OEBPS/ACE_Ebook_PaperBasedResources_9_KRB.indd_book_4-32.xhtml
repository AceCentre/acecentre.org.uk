Encoding can really help a communication partner to interpret your eye pointing, but it does take time to learn. Giving a communication partner the opportunity to practise is very helpful.

An encoded eye pointing chart does not have to involve colours and dots; you could use shading, shapes or numbers. The principle of grouping letters and making two movements to identify a letter remain the same.

This is an example of a simple eye pointing chart that has been encoded using numbers. To select the letter ‘B’ you would first eye point to the group of letters in the top left corner that contains the letters ‘A, B, C, D, E, F’. To clarify that ‘B’ was your desired letter, you would then eye point to the large number ‘2’ in the top centre, because ‘B’ is followed by the small number ‘2’.

If you wished to select the letter ‘B’, you would first eye point to the group that contains the characters ‘A, B, C, D, E, F’. To clarify that ‘B’ was your desired letter (rather than ‘A, C, D, E or F’), you would then eye point to the yellow dot, because ‘B’ is coloured yellow on the Frenchay E-tran frame.

Similarly, to select the letter ‘L’ you would first eye point to the group that contains the characters ‘G, H, I, J, K, L’. To clarify that ‘L’ was your desired letter, you would then eye point to or towards the red dot, because ‘L’ is coloured red.
