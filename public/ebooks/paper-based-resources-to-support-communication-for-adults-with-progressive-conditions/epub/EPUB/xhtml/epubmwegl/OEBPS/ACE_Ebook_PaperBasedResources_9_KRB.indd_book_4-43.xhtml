Making a Selection

As discussed above, all that is required to use listener mediated scanning is a single signal that indicates ‘yes, that’s the one I want’. There is no real need to say ‘no, not that one’ to options that you don’t want, as the communication partner can just assume it is a ‘no’, unless you respond ‘yes’. However, as always, it is down to personal preference.

Using only a ‘yes’ signal makes things much quicker as the communication partner doesn’t have to wait for a response after each letter. In addition, you can relax until the desired option is seen and / or heard which can be less tiring than having to communicate ‘no’ repeatedly.

The downside of this approach is that if your communication partner misses your ‘yes’ signal, you will then have to wait for them to go through the alphabet, or section of the alphabet, a second time. Such problems tend to arise with communication partners who are less familiar with the strategy, who may rush through the options or not look out for your signal. As discussed previously, practise can really help.

If a communication partner is really struggling, you might want to consider using the second signal that indicates ‘no, not that one’. After each letter is offered, the communication partner must wait for either a ‘yes’ or a ‘no’ signal from you before offering the next letter. It will slow things down but might just help someone who is prone to rushing or forgetting to look out for your signal. Hopefully as they gain in confidence, you can revert to just using the single ‘yes’ signal if you prefer.

It can help to write the instructions on how the communication partner should use the chart on the chart itself. Make sure the signals you are using to communicate ‘yes’ and ‘no’ (if relevant) are also documented.

Tips for Communication Partners When Using Listener Mediated Scanning

See Chapter 2 for tips to help communication partners more generally.

Pen and paper (or similar) can really help the communication partner keep track of what is being communicated, and you will have seen Katherine making use of this in the video of Katherine and Michael getting going with listener mediated scanning above.

If listener mediated scanning is being done with a chart, one trick is to stick a self-adhesive dry erase sheet onto the back of the chart as shown in the video by John Costello above.

When the communication partner is speaking aloud and / or pointing to letters, they need to ensure they are giving you enough time to respond. Try and encourage your communication partners to check with you that they are going at the correct speed.

It can sometimes be hard for a communication partner to juggle looking at the letters on the chart and watching you for a yes response, particularly if your ‘yes’ doesn’t not involve a sound. Be reassured that this does get easier as communication partners become more familiar with the letters on the chart and so can give you more of their visual attention.

As discussed, encourage your communication partner to check whether you have more to say before putting the chart down so that you are not inadvertently cut off mid conversation.
