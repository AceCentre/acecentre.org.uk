Letter Layouts

The letters on an alphabet chart are often placed in alphabetical order, which can feel very familiar. It can also help communication partners work out what letter you are pointing to - if your finger / other body part is obscuring the letter itself, they can easily work it out by looking at the adjacent letters.

However, for a touch typist or a regular computer user, a QWERTY layout may feel more familiar and easier to use. A printed QWERTY alphabet chart does feel different from a keyboard though, so it may still be worth trying other options before making a decision.

Frequency LayoutsAnother alternative is to use a frequency layout. With a frequency layout, letters that are used frequently in English such as ‘E’ or ‘T’, are placed where they are quicker / easier for you to select, and letters that are used infrequently, such as ‘Q’ or ‘Z’, are placed further away.

This approach tends to be used with listener mediated scanning, but can be adapted for use with other access methods. There is no single standard frequency layout, and the letter arrangement chosen will vary according to the access method used.
