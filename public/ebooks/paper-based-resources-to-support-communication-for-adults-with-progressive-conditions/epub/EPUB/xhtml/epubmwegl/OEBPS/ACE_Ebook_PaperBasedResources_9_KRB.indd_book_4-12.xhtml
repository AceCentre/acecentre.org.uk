Communication, by definition, involves two or more people. When paper-based tools and strategies are introduced into the act of communication, both you and your communication partner(s) will need to make accommodations and changes to the way you communicate.

Key Communication Partner Skills

Allow Time and Don’t RushA communication partner will need to accept that communicating using the kind of tools and strategies discussed in this guide is going to take a little more time than using speech. Janet, who is one of Beatrice’s carers, explains that people have, “just got to give the time to use [the whiteboard] so Beatrice can say what she needs to say to people.”

Lorraine has found that she too needs to slow down in order to communicate effectively using eye pointing. For her, what works best is to try and think letter-by-letter. She explains that this stops her thoughts from racing on ahead of what she is able to communicate.

Understand How You Communicate ‘Yes’ and ‘No’This sounds so simple, but if they aren’t clear on this, then everything else is going to get very confusing very fast! If you use a personalised way of communicating ‘yes’ and ‘no’, like blinking or making a specific movement, for example, you may want to make a point of demonstrating this to new communication partners.

Learn How Your Communication System WorksOne of the most helpful things a communication partner can do is to take the time to fully understand how the system that you are using works. If you are simply writing in a notebook it is fairly straightforward, but for some systems a bit more knowledge and understanding is required.

Beatrice uses a bespoke communication system based on combination access. She eye points to groups of letters written in permanent ink in the corners of a whiteboard, and then is offered relevant letters in turn from the group that Beatrice selects from. Her communication partner then writes the selected letter using a dry erase pen in the central section of the whiteboard.

Her carers Janet and Helen know the system inside out as they spend so much time with Beatrice and know her so well. Janet knows the letters by heart, for example, and doesn’t really need to look at the whiteboard to know what Beatrice is selecting. However, it is a way of communicating that many haven’t encountered before, and new communication partners need training on how it all works. New carers are trained on the use of the whiteboard and, after watching Janet or Helen use the system with Beatrice, are encouraged to chat to Beatrice themselves to gain confidence and experience. This takes time and perseverance, but the time spent practising helps them to gain confidence with the system.

Sometimes it is helpful for a communication partner to be given time to practise the system you are using away from the pressure of an important conversation. You might begin by spelling out an agreed word and then progress to them trying to find out something fun like the name of your first pet, for example. They might also find it helpful to try using the tool themselves.

It’s not just the mechanics of how you select letters that your communication partner needs to learn. They need to understand rules like how you will indicate you have something to say in the first place, how you will signal a space, or how you will let them know that something’s gone wrong. These rules are often the difference between conversation flowing and communication breakdown.
