One way of having fun with choice making is to ask the individual to make a choice on behalf of someone else! Maybe they could choose what Dad has for a snack for example. Depending on how daring the person being chosen for is, you could put some funny items in amongst the options e.g. biscuit, banana, sock, with Dad pretending to munch the sock that is chosen for him.

You could also encourage a child to make choices for a doll or teddy. What does dolly want to wear? What does teddy want to drink? Again, this approach introduces the concept of choice without introducing any risk of failure.

About the Example Charts

The example charts here are one small step on the journey towards developing a full communication system based around symbols. We would not anticipate that every chart would be of interest to every individual! It is about finding one or two that might capture the imagination of the individual.

When getting started, the charts are there to be used by you, the communicaton partner. If the person who will be using symbols begins to join in, that’s great, but don’t force them to use the symbols. They need to become really familiar with the symbols and how to use them which can only happen by you using them yourself as you talk to them.

You really are the key to getting started with AAC. Your skill at pointing to the symbols while talking to and engaging with them is what will get AAC going. You can read much more about this in Chapter 5, Your Role as a Communication Partner.

The example charts are designed to be accessed by pointing to the symbols. However, you could cut the symbols out and arrange them on an E-tran frame for eye pointing communicators. You could also scan through the symbols for those using listener mediated scanning, although make sure you are consistent in the order in which you offer the symbols.

The communication charts shown below are all made using PCS symbols. However, on the ACE Centre website you can also download the equivalent charts made with Widgit and Symbolstix symbols.

There are a set of words that we use across huge numbers of different situations – these are known as core words. This chapter contains some example core vocabulary charts. Then there are many different examples of charts that have been created to support specific activities. However, these charts all contain some core vocabulary too.

Some of the charts have examples of things you could say using the symbols. This is just to give you a flavour of the range of things you can say. It is not intended to be prescriptive, and certainly isn’t comprehensive! A few charts also have video examples illustrating their use. The examples given highlight the way that symbols allow you to communicate rich ideas. The goal is not perfectly grammatical sentences.

Try and choose a chart that feels a step ahead of where you think the individual is at languagewise. This will enable you to demonstrate the next steps as you use the chart. It’s what we do all the time with speaking children – we repeat back what they say, adding an extra word. We are all natural language teachers! You can always blank off a few symbols if it feels too much and gradually reveal them. You can read more about this approach here.
