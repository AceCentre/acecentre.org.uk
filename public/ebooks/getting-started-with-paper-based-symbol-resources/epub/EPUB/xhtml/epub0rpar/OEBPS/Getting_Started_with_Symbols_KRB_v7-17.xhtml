Chapter 2 – More About Symbols

Learning Symbols

People who are new to the world of symbols often worry that they look hard to learn and feel concerned that they will be too difficult.

Some words or concepts are very easy to draw or represent as a picture. For example, it’s pretty easy to draw a picture of an elephant that looks like an elephant. Other words are much more difficult to draw. For example, there is no obvious picture of “more” or “help” or “finish”.

Frustratingly, it is the words that don’t make pictures easily that are often the most important when we communicate. Elephants do come up in conversation occasionally, but words like “more”, “help”, and “finish” are ones we use every day no matter what we are talking about. They are also the sort of words that young children start using very early on, and therefore the sort of words that we want to get started with straight away using AAC. As they are so important, we tend to refer to these words as core words. You can read more about core words and vocabulary more generally in Chapter 3.

If we only make charts using the words that are easy to draw, we are seriously limiting the language development of the young person communicating through AAC. Right from the very beginning, we need to include the important core words to give children the very best chance of communicating effectively. We don’t just list objects when we talk, and it’s important that we don’t just give people using AAC charts full of the easy-to-draw objects. You will see plenty of core words included in the example charts in Chapter 4.

Research does endorse this approach. Researchers have suggested that usefulness of the symbol is more important than how easy the picture is to learn when it comes to people learning to use the symbol. And a word like ‘more’ is certainly more useful than ‘elephant’ on a day to day basis!

To help people learn to use these symbols, we need to use them ourselves as often as possible when talking to them. This is the very best way of making them easier to learn. See Chapter 5: Your Role as a Communication Partner, for more information.

The Role of Photographs

There are no absolute rules about when to use photographs and when to use symbols. Photographs can be easier to learn, and a set of photographs of specific objects and people in someone’s environment might be a useful support to their communication. Indeed this may be a stage on an individual’s journey towards using symbols.

For some on the autistic spectrum, photo-based charts can also be very helpful for talking about emotions.

Even if someone is using a communication system based on symbols, photographs can still have a valuable role to play. While a symbol of ‘friend’ is ideal for talking about the concept of having a friend, when talking about a specific friend called Sally, a photograph of Sally herself may be more useful. Similarly, the family dog Patch is probably best represented using a photograph of Patch. However, it is important to also have a symbol of a ‘dog’ so that someone can communicate about other dogs. For example, they might want to say “I like dogs” rather than “I like Patch.”

Photographs can have another role to play in communication. They are a great way to share stories about people, activities and events.

However photographs are being used, it is helpful to ensure they are as clear as possible, particularly as they are often quite small when used on a communication chart.
