Chapter 5 – Your Role as a Communication Partner

Respond to What is Communicated

Learning to communicate using symbols takes time and effort. If someone does manage to use their symbols to communicate something it is important to value this communication and respond to it. That’s not to say that you have to hand over a bar of chocolate every time someone points to the chocolate symbol! However, it is important to acknowledge that they have asked for chocolate and either give it to them or explain that it is not possible now. Try not to ignore anything that is communicated, even if it seems a bit odd or inconvenient.

It can be helpful to confirm to someone what you are responding to and why. Describe what they are doing to give them feedback. For example, if someone pointed to the “more” symbols whilst building a tower you could say “you pointed to ‘more’ – here’s another block for your tower”, or perhaps, “You looked up to say ‘yes – that’s what I want’. Let’s go and get it.”

Praise

You can also incorporate praise into your feedback. For example, “You pointed to the train symbol! Great talking! Here’s the train.” or “You pointed to ‘more’ to ask for more television – well done.” Find language that feels comfortable for you. It doesn’t all have to be about superlatives – the greats, the goods, the bests(!) – it’s just about showing someone that you are noticing that they are using symbols and that you value what they are doing.

Describing what someone is doing within your through praise can highlight the behaviour that you want to encourage. Such descriptive praise can be a great tool for encouraging more of the same behaviour.

Self Talk

Using symbol-based AAC requires a lot of problem solving and complex skills. As you are using the AAC yourself, try and speak aloud some of the thinking you are doing in your head – your self talk. This provides a simple commentary on what you’re thinking and doing.

Self talk helps people learn how the symbols work and how to use them. You might say for example, “I want to ask you about your brother. I think I’d better go to your people page.” Or perhaps, “I’ve had enough singing for today, I’m pointing to ‘stop’ to tell you it’s time to stop singing.”

Testing, Testing

At school, and even outside of formal educational environments, people often feel the need to test the abilities of people who communicate using AAC. Of course there is a place for test and assessments. However, it is important that we don’t make every communicative interaction a test.

One of the ways in which people are tempted to test people who use symbol-based AAC is to move their symbols around to see if they can still find them. This is a bit like someone coming along and moving all the keys around on your computer keyboard and then asking you to show them what you can do! Consistent locations are so important. If you want someone to tell you how many lions they can see in the picture, do it using their familiar numbers chart – don’t jumble them up!

Stay Positive and Value the Communication Tools

By modelling and using the system yourself, you will already be showing the individual how important you think their communication system is. However, if you slip into the habit of making negative comments about the symbols or chart in front of the person using them, they are likely to pick up on this. It is so easily done in a moment of frustration if a symbol is missing or there is a problem with the chart.
