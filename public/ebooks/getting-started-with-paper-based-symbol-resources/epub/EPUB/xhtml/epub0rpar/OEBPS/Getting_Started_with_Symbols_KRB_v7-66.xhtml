Experimenting with spacing

You may need to help someone learn that one finger is in charge of “pointing”. Sometimes a sticker or some sparkly nail varnish on the pointing fingernail can help. For those comfortable wearing them, cutting the end off the pointing finger of an old glove can also work.

If someone is struggling to point clearly, handheld pointing tools / styli might be considered. There are many different options that can be explored e.g. see dadinashed.com.

Another thing to think about is placing the chart or book at an angle rather than flat on a table or tray – a writing slope or angled wedge can help. Securing the chart or book on a flat or angled surface with a sticky fixer like Blue Tack or a non-slip material like Dycem or Tenura can also make a difference. It’s amazing how much easier it is to point to something that isn’t sliding around!

Visual Accessiblity

Visual difficulties can have a significant impact on someone’s ability to make use of a symbol-based chart or communication book. Symbols and symbol charts can be busy and detailed and visual skills play a huge part in learning what symbols mean and how to use them. However, visual difficulties can be poorly understood. It is helpful to ask lots of questions about someone’s functional vision, seek any relevant reports and make careful observations.

Designing a visually accessible chart can make a big difference to its useability. As discussed in Chapter 2, this can be done by considering the choice of symbols and, as discussed in Chapter 6, the colour scheme of the chart. Thicker borders and the use of space may also help visual clarity.

Careful positioning either in terms of distance from the eyes or placement within someone’s visual field may also help to make a resource more accessible.

One method of bringing a symbol into the optimum place in someone’s visual field is to use a “pick-up-and-show” approach. Individual symbols are laminated and velcroed to a chart. The communication partner then picks up the symbol and shows it to the individual, holding it in the optimum place. This approach can support the modelling of symbols. It can also be used with listener mediated scanning.
