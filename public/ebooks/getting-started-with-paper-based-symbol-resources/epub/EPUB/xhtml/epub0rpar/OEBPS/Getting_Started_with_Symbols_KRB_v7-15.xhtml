Although Makaton®, PCS (Picture Communication Symbols ®), SymbolStix and Widgit symbols, are perhaps the most commonly used, there are others available. Other sets that are sometimes seen include Bliss symbols, Pictogram symbols, Clarity symbols, amongst others. There are also a number of freely available symbol sets around, many of which can be found on OpenSymbols. The Australian company Spectronics has produced a useful comparison of symbol sets.

All symbol sets have their strengths and weaknesses, and it is worth spending time working out which set is most likely to meet the needs of the person you are supporting and their environment.

When choosing a symbol set for use in a person’s paper-based communication system it is important to take into account what symbols (if any) they are using elsewhere. For example, if they have an electronic communication aid or are using symbol-based software or apps, you may be able to use the same symbols in their paper-based system. It may be worth checking with staff at an early years setting, school, college, day centre, or similar environment that someone attends to find out if a symbol set is established there. If possible, it is helpful to be consistent so that someone is not having to learn multiple symbol sets. Having said that, people often end up mixing and matching to some degree, ‘borrowing’ missing symbols from other sets when needed.

Whatever the symbol set chosen, do include the written word alongside the symbol. This will help support literacy development and will also make it easier for an unfamiliar communication partner to understand the message.

Colour or Black and White Symbols

With the exception of the Makaton® symbol library, which is exclusively black and white, most symbol libraries are drawn in full colour. However, some resource making software will let you convert coloured symbols into black and white. Whether you use colour or black and white versions really comes down to the needs of the individual.

Four full colour PCS Symbols alongside their black and white versions

Colour can help someone to identify the meaning of the symbol. In the example above, the fact that the juice in the glass and the circular fruit next to it are coloured orange make it more obvious that the symbol represents orange juice. Colour may also assist someone in locating a symbol on a busy chart as the use of colour provides more differentiation between each symbol.

However, for some, the colour in the symbol may be visually distracting, and the use of black and white may make the symbol appear cleaner and easier to see. For someone on the autistic spectrum with a strong preference for or dislike of a specific colour, then use of that colour in the symbol may even get in the way of them using the symbol communicatively.
