For example, when Diane Millar, Janice Light and Ralph Schlosser analysed the literature in 2006, they found that following the introduction of AAC, no cases demonstrated a decrease in speech production, some showed no change, and most demonstrated gains in speech production.

Would you worry that someone who is wearing glasses will become lazy about using their eyes? Probably not! It really is the same with AAC. AAC simply provides a set of tools that can support someone’s language and communication.

For some, use of AAC will be part of their journey towards speech. Whilst speech is developing, it can underpin the development of their language and communication skills. This means that language isn’t ‘put on hold’ until speech comes, risking long term deficits or delays. For others, use of AAC will always be part of their lives. Either way, putting the work in now will only help someone to be a good communicator, however this is done, in the future.

You will find a good summary of the issues and research around the use of AAC on TobiiDynavox’s website. If you would like to read some of the journal articles around this area, PrAACticalAAC.org have put together a useful list of relevant articles.

Who Uses AAC?

AAC is used by people of all ages and abilities – from young toddlers to the elderly, from people with advanced degrees to those with learning difficulties.

There are many different medical reasons why people need to use AAC to support or replace their speech. A person may have been born with a condition such as cerebral palsy or a syndrome such as Down’s syndrome or Angelman syndrome, have autism or an autistic spectrum condition, have a learning disability, have difficulties with speech and language following a head injury, stroke or brain tumour, have developed a progressive neurological condition… the list is long.

What unites this incredibly diverse group of people is the fact that their speech does not enable them to communicate in all situations or with all individuals without some extra support.

AAC takes a lot of work to get started and a lot of work to develop and use over time. But it really is worth it! Communication is what defines us and makes us who we are. It is how we make friends and develop and maintain relationships. It gets us what we want (well, some of the time anyway) and it enables us to be who we want to be.

Melinda Smith, a Community Development Worker in Australia who uses paper-based and electronic AAC to support her communication, has said:

“Using AAC makes me feel like I exist, it gives me the power to make a contribution and it enables more people to communicate with me. Without AAC – I may as well be asleep.” (from www.toby-churchill.com)
