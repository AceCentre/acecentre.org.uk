In this chapter you will find lots of examples of resources you could use to get started. Alternatively you could seek inspiration from these but make your own.

Don’t Worry About Mistakes

When getting started, please don’t worry about mistakes! Making mistakes is part of how we learn.

Toddlers don’t open their mouths and start speaking perfectly formed intelligible sentences. In the same way, a person learning to use AAC will need time to learn how to use symbols to support their communication and will inevitably make mistakes along the way. If communication goes a bit wrong, don’t worry. Just use it as an opportunity to learn.

Sometimes expectations of how quickly AAC is going to work can be a bit high! It is helpful to see it as more akin to learning a new language.

Think About Starting with ‘No Fail’ Activities

Getting started with AAC needs to be fun for everyone. In time, the use of AAC will hopefully develop into a robust communication system that can be used to talk about problems, do maths homework, and to have an argument about how much television it’s ok to watch! But in the beginning, we need to try and keep it fun and motivating. No fail activites are a great place to start.

No fail activities are activities where you can’t get anything “wrong”. For example, if you are playing a game where the person using AAC has to point to a set of symbols where each one tells you to do a different silly thing like in the example Simon Says chart, there is nothing that they can get wrong. It’s a no fail activity that is just good fun. Whatever is chosen results in a fun and motivating outcome, or an opportunity for a nice chat together.

By contrast, if the person is given a chart of food options and asked to point to the symbol of the food item that you are holding up, this can lead to a feeling of having “got something wrong” if they don’t point to the correct symbol. To keep frustration at bay it is helpful to lift the pressure, particularly when getting started. That is why most of the example charts are “no fail”.

Choice Making

Giving choices through AAC can be very empowering. For someone with a complex communication disability alongside a complex physical disability, it can be a way that they begin to take control of their environment and those around them. This is so important for learning.

However, some people can find making choices a bit stressful. It is worth remembering that there are instances where someone might not actually know which option they want, or not really mind either way. Alternatively, having made a definite choice, they may then change their mind! When getting started with AAC, this can lead to confusion. It may seems as though they don’t understand the AAC system when in fact they have simply changed their mind. Try not to put too much pressure when presenting them with choices. It’s so easily done!

You may also have evolved other ways of making choices. It might just be quicker to hold up the two flavours of crisps on offer for example, and then give them the one that they look at or reach for. That’s ok too. Not everything has to be done through a formal AAC system.

If you do decide to introduce choice making when getting started, try and keep it fun and light hearted.
