Symbols

Symbols are specially drawn pictures that are used to represent concepts. For example, a drawing of a person drinking out of a cup can be used to represent the concept of having a drink. In the photograph above, the nursery classroom is represented by a symbol to the left of the teddy bear object of reference. As the classroom is a place, the drawing of the child and toys is surrounded by an outline in the shape of a house. Within that symbol set (PCS ®), the special house shape is used on all symbols that represent places.

Again, symbols are used both to support understanding and to help someone express themselves.

You can read much more about symbols in Chapter 2, and designing, supporting and developing paper-based symbol resources is the focus of this guide.

Whole Words and the Alphabet

For individuals who are able to read, whole words and phrases can be used instead of symbols or objects to represent meaning. For those able to spell, alphabet charts can be used, enabling someone to generate words and phrases spontaneously. You can find out more about designing and using alphabet charts in Designing and Using Alphabet Charts – Getting Started with AAC.

Alphabet charts are also able to support literacy development and should be used alongside other communication tools at all stages of communication ability. You can read more about this later in the guide.

A Multi-Modal Approach to AAC

No one form of AAC is ‘better’ than another, and successful use of one form is not a prerequisite to a successful use of another. In reality, most people take a multi-modal approach, combining paper-based AAC with a host of other communication tools and strategies, including gesture, signing, vocalisation and electronic AAC, in a way that works best for them.
