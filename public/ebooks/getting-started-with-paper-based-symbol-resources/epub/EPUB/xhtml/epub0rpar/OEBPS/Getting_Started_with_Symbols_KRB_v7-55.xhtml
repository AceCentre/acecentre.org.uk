Chapter 5 – Your Role as a Communication Partner

People need to experience success with their symbol-based AAC and have lots of positive experiences when using it. The resources in Chapter 4 may give you some ideas of games or activities that might capture their interest.

You may need to create opportunities where AAC has maximum impact. For example, with some forward planning, someone could decide where everyone sits at lunch time, or a child could use AAC to tell their classmates what action to do in a PE class.

Developing Language through Modelling

When parents and carers talk to young children, instinctively they tend to keep their speech simple and “one step ahead” of that of the child. We can use similar skills to support the language development of symbol users.

Try and keep your sentences short and simple, and don’t point to too many symbols at once.

To help someone who is beginning to use symbols to move on, you can repeat back what they have said, expanding on what they have pointed to. For example, if a child points to “more” on a train play chart to ask for another train, you could respond, “you’re asking for more trains”, while pointing to “more” and “train” – and then, of course, give them the train they have asked for! You have both responded to their request, and modelled how they could develop it further.

The other way in which language can be developed through modelling is by you bringing an alphabet chart into as many activities as possible. As discussed in Chapter 4, alphabet charts can be brought into activities well in advance of them being used communicatively by an individual. You might point to the first letter of the name of the person who has just walked into the room for example or point to the first letter of the chosen food item. Like with symbols, it is all about using the alphabet within meaningful and motivating activites.
