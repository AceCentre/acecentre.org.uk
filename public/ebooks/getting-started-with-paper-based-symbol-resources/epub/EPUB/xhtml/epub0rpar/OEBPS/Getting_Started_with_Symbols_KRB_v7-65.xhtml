Chapter 7

﻿Using Paper-based AAC if Pointing is Difficult

In this chapter:

An introduction to ways people with physical disabilities can use paper-based AAC, including:

making pointing easier

visual accessibility

eye pointing

listener mediated scanning

The aim of this chapter is to provide a very brief introduction to how people can access and use paper-based AAC resources when pointing is difficult. This information is explored in detail within the complementary guide Access to paper-based symbol resources when pointing is difficult – Getting Started with AAC.

Something to bear in mind when thinking about access to paper-based AAC resources is that people may have more than one way of doing it. For example, someone might be able to point to symbols most of the time but need another access method when they are unwell, or find that they can use one way of selecting symbols in the morning but need another access method as fatigue sets in.

Making Pointing Easier

Sometimes pointing to symbols can be made easier by simply changing the size of the symbols used and the number of symbols on a page. You might also avoid placing symbols in an area of the page that someone finds difficult to point to and ensure that the most useful symbols are placed where it is easiest.

Increasing the amount of space between each symbol can also help when someone finds it difficult to clearly point to one item. For example, here are two versions of a chart for playing with balloons. It’s not a big change, but the second example has more space between the cells. This could make it easier for someone who is using their whole hand to select, for example, or who finds targeting difficult.
