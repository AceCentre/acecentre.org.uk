Chapter 5 – Your Role as a Communication Partner

The great news is that it’s not hard to do – it is far easier than learning to speak another language. You don’t even need to learn the symbols yourself as you can rely on the text labels. You can see Clare modelling how to use the symbols whilst playing bubbles in the video below.

Modelling is a long term undertaking. Please don’t be disheartened if someone doesn’t start joining in straight away. It is going to take time for them to learn what communicating through symbols is all about. Forcing them to point to symbols will only put them off. Just keep using the symbols yourself and showing them how much fun communicating with symbols can be.

If you set out to point to symbols all day every day it will probably feel like too big an undertaking. One way to get going is by trying to find a smaller amount of focused time each day. You can then gradually increase the amount of time and situations within which you use symbols.

Observe and Watch

When modelling, it is particularly powerful if you are commenting on something the person is doing or engaged by. Rather than rushing in, watch them and follow their lead. For example, if they are enjoying a television programme and laughing, you could comment, “You like this”, while pointing to a “like” symbol. If they are playing with a doll and looking at the pushchair, you could comment, “you want the pushchair” while pointing to “want”, and then go and get it for them.

Wait and Allow Time

Waiting is a very powerful communication partner skill. After you’ve done some point talking, you pause and give the person time to contribute. It can feel a bit unnatural at first (sometimes counting silently to 5 helps). You can signal to the person that you expect them to respond using your body language, perhaps looking at them and at the chart.

Don’t be disheartened if they don’t join in straight away; just carry on pointing to the symbols yourself.

Remember that communicating using AAC takes more time than communicating using speech. Try and relax. Accept that there will be quiet moments in the conversation and don’t rush to fill these with more questions. This is a sure fire route to confusion as you may then end up in a muddle as to which question the answer relates to! It can also result in someone missing their slot in the conversation.

Maximum Impact

Communicating using symbols is harder work and not as straightforward as talking. People learning to use symbols need to understand that it’s worth the effort! Try and introduce symbol-based AAC into contexts where it gives someone real control over their play, activities or people in their world.
