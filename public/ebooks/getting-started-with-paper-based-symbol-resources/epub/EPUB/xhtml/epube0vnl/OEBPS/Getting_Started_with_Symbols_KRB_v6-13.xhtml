Chapter 1 – An Introduction to AAC

Tamsin has cerebral palsy and is not able to use speech to communicate. Before she was able to rely upon spelling, she communicated by eye pointing to symbols.

When she was three, Tamsin wore two wristbands – one with a symbol for ‘yes’ on it, the other with a symbol for ‘no’, and she began to respond to simple questions by looking at the appropriate band. She also had a collection of symbols that she could eye point to.

This collection of symbols was formalised into a full communication book which quickly contained pages and pages of symbols, and access to lots of complex language.

Tamsin was able to use her symbols to play, join in with discussions at her mainstream primary school, and tease her brother! In the picture above

﻿Tamsin

(taken when she was seven) she is playing Mr Potato Head with her younger brother.

Tamsin’s communication system was so valued by her peers that when she was seven, she was nominated to represent them on the school council. Alongside her eye pointing communication book (which went EVERYWHERE with her), Tamsin also used a voice output communication aid.

This video, taken when Tamsin was just five years old, shows her sharing a story with a visitor using her communication book.

 

Tamsin shares a story with a visitor. Her Mum then shows Tamsin how she could expand her story. Tamsin accesses her communication book using eye pointing.

 

Tamsin now attends mainstream college and uses a communication system based predominantly around spelling. She remains a very determined young woman with a great sense of humour who enjoys joining in with everyone.
