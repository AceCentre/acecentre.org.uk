Chapter 8

﻿﻿Sourcing and Making Symbol Resources

In this chapter:

• All about sourcing and making symbol resources

This chapter shares information about sourcing and making paper-based symbol charts. You can find out more about resources to support the development of a communication book in the next chapter.

When printing paper-based charts (or pages for a communication book), you might want to either laminate them or print them out on specialist paper. Laminating a chart makes it more robust and stops it getting tatty. For those pointing with a finger, it also makes the surface more slippery which can help with sliding a finger around. Alternatively, you could print a chart on so-called “indestructible”, tear-proof or waterproof printer paper.

When laminating, bear in mind that there are two types of laminate pouch available – gloss and matt. Gloss laminate tends to be the most common and widely used. However, it does reflect overhead lighting which can make it harder for the person using the chart and their communication partner to see what is being pointed to. Matt laminate tends to work a bit better from this perspective and is just as robust and slippery.

The only proviso here is that if you are creating an eye pointing chart, gloss laminate is best as this is transparent once it has been through the laminator whereas matt laminate becomes slightly cloudy.

To locate resources made in languages other than English, you may want to consult the websites of specialist AAC centres in relevant countries. The International Society for Augmentative and Alternative Communication (isaac-online.org) may be a good place to start. The website lists their chapters around the world, each of whom has contact details. Eastern Health provider in Melbourne Australia has produced symbol-based resources (predominantly around medical needs) in many languages.
