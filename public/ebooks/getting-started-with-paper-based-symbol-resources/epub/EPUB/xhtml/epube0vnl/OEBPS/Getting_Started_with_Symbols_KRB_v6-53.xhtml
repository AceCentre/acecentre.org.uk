﻿Your Role as a Communication Partner

In this chapter:

Consider your role in supporting the introduction and development of symbol-based AAC

Look at different communication partner skills

Think about the role siblings and peers might play

When we talk about a communication partner we mean “you”! A communication partner is a person with whom the person who uses AAC communicates.

Communication is a two way process – therefore it is not just all about the skills of the person who uses AAC. When introducing AAC, of course the person using the symbols needs to learn a lot, but so too does the communication partner! The communication partner has a huge role to play in the success or otherwise of an AAC system. Where someone does not have skilled communication partners, it can be much, much harder for them to really get going with symbol-based AAC and indeed may result in them abandoning this way of communicating.

Modelling

The most important communication partner skill that you can learn is modelling. This is where you point to symbols while you talk. Essentially you are modelling how it’s done. We sometimes call it point-talking to emphasise the fact that you are pointing to symbols and talking at the same time.

Imagine what a challenge it would be if you were asked to speak French before you had ever heard French spoken yourself! Yet this is equivalent to the challenge we are setting children and adults who use symbol-based AAC when we hand over a chart full of symbols and expect them to use it without ever having seen anyone else use one.

Babies and toddlers are surrounded by speech long before they start using it themselves. They learn the words through them being spoken around them, and they learn the power of speech through seeing what it achieves. We need to give people who used symbol-based AAC some of the same opportunities. We need to surround them with symbols, and use them ourselves when we talk to them. In this way they can learn what the symbols mean and what they can achieve. They can also see that we value symbols as we take the time to use them ourselves.
