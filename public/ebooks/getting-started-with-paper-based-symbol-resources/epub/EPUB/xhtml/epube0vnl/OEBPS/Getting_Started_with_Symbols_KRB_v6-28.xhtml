Chapter 4 – Ideas and Resources for Getting Going

Playing with Toys

Play is a great opportunity for language learning and development. The key is to watch what toys someone enjoys and try to bring AAC into that activity. The charts provided aim to give ideas but you may need to make your own.

AAC can make toys a lot more interesting. If someone has a physical disability alongside their communication disability, AAC may give them real control over their play for the first time.

When Tamsin was very young she had a doll that was not of much interest to her. She had a doll symbol so she could choose to play with it, but no other symbols around doll play. She rarely chose that symbol and the doll was not played with very much. Once she was introduced to a communication chart that enabled her to play with the doll, all that changed.

Her doll play communication chart contained symbols like “wash”, “change”, “sleep”, and “bottle”, alongside core symbols like “more” and “stop”. Tamsin’s Mum got the doll out and began to point to the symbols while playing with the doll to show Tamsin what they meant and how to use them (modelling). Tamsin soon joined in too.

With the help of her Mum, she played and played with the doll! By eye pointing to the “change” symbol, Tamsin told her Mum that the doll needed a new outfit. Together they changed her clothes. By eye pointing to the “bottle” symbol, she told her Mum that the doll was hungry. Mum helped her to hold the bottle to give the doll a feed. By pointing to the “sleep” symbol, she told Mum that the doll was ready for a nap, and together they tucked her up in bed. You can see Tamsin playing with the doll here.

For a time, she had a strong bond with the doll and it went everywhere with her. Tamsin’s play was enriched by having access to a symbol chart that gave her the language needed to play with the doll.

Turn Taking

A lot of playing with toys and games involves turn taking. You could bring in a simple chart to so many activities which just enables you to talk about whose turn it is, or who should be having a turn! This personalised example allows someone to talk about when it is Mummy’s turn and when it is Edward’s turn.
