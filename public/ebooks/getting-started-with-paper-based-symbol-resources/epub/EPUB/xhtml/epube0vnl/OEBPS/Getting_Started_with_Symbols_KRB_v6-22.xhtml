Chapter 3 – Choosing Vocabulary

Core words consist of a wide range of grammatical forms – i.e. action words, describing words, etc. On the second café chart above, the core words are shown with a thicker border.

As discussed in Chapter 2, it is not always easy to draw a picture of a core word. The difficulty of drawing a picture to represent concepts like “more” or “want” should not deter us from including these core words in symbol-based AAC. With the right support from communication partners, people can learn to understand and use these abstract symbols.

Indeed with some very short core words, it is arguably as easy to learn the shape of the written word as the abstract symbol. In these situations, you may just want to teach the actual word rather than the symbol. The word can be introduced and taught in the same way as the symbol would be.

Fringe

Fringe words are the words that are topic or situation specific. For example, words like “snack”, “spread”, “plate”, “sauce” are useful at snack time, or if you are discussing food or planning a meal, but not so useful if you are watching TV. Fringe words tend to be nouns (things) and are much easier to draw a picture of.

However, as the café example illustrated, if we rely solely on fringe words when producing charts and books, we can end up with something that is very limited in its usefulness.

On the café chart above, the fringe words have the thinner border, and relate specifically to the café situation – e.g. tea, coffee, milk, sugar, etc.

Core and Fringe Vocabulary

When thinking about vocabulary (or words) within AAC, we tend to divide it into core and fringe.

Core words are words that are useful whatever the situation whereas fringe words are topic or situation specific. For example, words like “more”, “help”, “not”, “like”, “look”, “go” and “want” are relevant whether you are playing with bubbles, doing an art project or off on a trip to the shops and are examples of core words. By contrast, “bubble”, “crayon” or “trolley” are examples of fringe words.

In an ideal world, a symbol-based communication chart or book will contain a mixture of core and fringe words.

Core

Research shows that speaking toddlers rely predominantly on core words to communicate, and that a similar set of core words is used by speakers of all ages.
