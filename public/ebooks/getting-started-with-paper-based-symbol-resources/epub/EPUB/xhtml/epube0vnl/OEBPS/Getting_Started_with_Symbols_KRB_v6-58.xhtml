Chapter 5 – Your Role as a Communication Partner

Keep your own language simple. But don’t fall into the trap of just asking questions that someone can answer “yes” or “no” to – this makes for a rather boring conversation for both parties! Try to just introduce one topic of conversation at a time.

Module Four of the freely available online learning resources on www.aacscotland.org.uk provides more detailed information about being a good communication partner, and offers some excellent downloadable resources.

Communication Matters have developed a useful leaflet called Speaking with Someone Who Uses AAC. You can read the leaflet here.

Siblings and Peers

When we think about communication partners, we tend to think of the adults around the person using symbols. However, there is another type of communication partner who may be forgotten about, particularly in relation to children, and that is any siblings!

Some siblings love to be got involved in supporting the child who uses AAC and relish the challenge of learning how to use symbols themselves. If they are very young, they can learn how to communicate using AAC alongside learning to talk. This can only enhance their own communication skills as is illustrated in this video of Tamsin and her brother.
