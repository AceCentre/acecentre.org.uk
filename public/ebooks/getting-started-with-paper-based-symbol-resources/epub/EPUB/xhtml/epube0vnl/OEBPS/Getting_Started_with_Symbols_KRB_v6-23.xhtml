As someone’s communication system grows and develops, there may be more space available for these symbols.

Multilingual Resources

There can sometimes be a need to produce symbol-based AAC resources in more than one language. One strategy is to produce text labels in each language above or below the symbol. This may also assist acommunication partner who might understand one but not both of the languages in question.

If you are developing resources in a language with which you are unfamiliar, it is always worth consulting a native speaker. Online translation tools cannot always be relied upon and will not pick up cultural differences in the use of vocabulary.

Chapter 3 – Choosing Vocabulary

Yes and No

You will see that the example charts provided in Chapter 4 don’t include the symbols / words for “yes” and “no”. This is because people often have their own preferred ways of communicating “yes” and “no” that do not involve the use of symbols. It is also important that someone is able to communicate “yes” and “no” when they don’t have access to symbols.

However, there are times when you may want to think about including “yes” and “no” on a chart, particularly if the someone’s own method is not always clear to unfamiliar communication partners. In such cases, where a communication partner is left feeling unsure, it can be useful to have a back up way of communicating these important concepts.

Alongside “yes” and “no”, it is useful for the someone to have a clear way of indicating “I don’t know”. Again, if this cannot always be clearly communicated, it might be worth including an “I don’t know” symbol.

A Note on Manners

Manners have an important cultural role to play in communication. Therefore having “please” and “thank you” on a communication chart from the very beginning can feel important. This is a very personal matter, and there are no hard and fast rules!

One note of caution though is that when there is only limited space on a chart, it may be worth leaving out “please” and “thank you” for a time. They are not always the most motivating words to use, and do take up valuable space on a chart.

If you are feeling worried about leaving these symbols off the chart, you could always add them in yourself – for example if someone points to “more” to ask for more bubbles, you could say “more please” as you blow some more bubbles, so that they are being exposed to the concept.
