Chapter 6 – Designing Symbol Resources – More to Think About

Consistency

When creating communication charts or books it is helpful to be as consistent as possible in your placement of symbols, particularly symbols that are repeated across resources. This enables people to use their motor learning when selecting symbols.

Do you remember learning to use a QWERTY keyboard? It probably seemed to take forever to find a letter. Whereas after a while you find your fingers automatically moving towards a letter without too much thought. A symbol chart is a little bit like a keyboard layout in that respect. Moving symbols around can mean that approaching a communication chart is a bit like approaching a mixed up keyboard layout every time.

By contrast, if when designing a chart the symbol ‘more’ is always in the top left hand corner, for example, someone can begin to learn it is there and their hand can begin to travel towards it without them having to search for it or give it too much thought. This motor learning is known as automaticity.

Another thing that can help with motor learning is to plan for the future when designing a communication chart. If you feel that someone can only cope with ten symbols for now but that you would like to move them on in due course, one option is to create a chart with ten cells now and then a

chart with twenty cells later. However, as in the example below, another option is to create a chart with twenty cells now, and simply blank out ten of them – in whatever pattern works for you. This means that as the number of symbols is increased, the person does not have to re-learn the location of symbols.
