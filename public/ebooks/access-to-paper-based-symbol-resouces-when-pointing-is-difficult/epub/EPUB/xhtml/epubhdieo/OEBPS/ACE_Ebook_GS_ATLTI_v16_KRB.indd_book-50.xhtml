﻿Presenting the Options

When getting started with visual and auditory scanning or auditory scanning, it can be helpful to speak aloud the options first so that the individual knows what’s coming and can think about their response. Then read aloud each option slowly and carefully, pausing for long enough after each option to give the individual time to respond. The speed and duration of pause will be entirely dependent upon the needs of the individual.

If you are using visual scanning, again make sure that you pause for long enough after pointing to each option to give the individual enough time to respond.

With all aproaches, you may need to go through the list more than once to give the individual time to think through what they want to say.

As you both become more experienced, you may not need to go through all the options first as these will be known. You will also become more experienced at gauging the amount of time you need to pause after delivering each option. This information can then be included in the instructions.

If you are using visual and auditory scanning or auditory scanning, it is worth thinking about the way you use your voice to do this. Try and present the options using a neutral voice. Keep expression to a minimum, and use as low a volume as is appropriate to the individual and the setting. When a choice is made, speak aloud the option that has been chosen using a more typical social speaking voice. The option chosen should sound more animated than the more neutral presentation of the options.

You can hear the different uses of voice in the video above that demonstrates listener mediated scanning of the bubbles chart. Katharine speaks aloud the options in a calm and quiet voice, but once a selection has been made, she speaks it aloud using a more animated voice.

Smart Partner

The joy of a paper-based scanning system (i.e. one that is delivered by a communication partner rather than a computer) is that you are so much cleverer than a computer! If you are reading through a list and there is a sudden distraction, you know to pause and allow time for that to pass. If the individual is tired that day, you know to read through the options more slowly. You may also be able to spot a ‘yes’ response that is being initiated, even if it’s not quite on target due to fatigue or illness.

But sometimes partners aren’t so smart. With scanning systems it is incredibly important to ensure that all communication partners understand how to use the system and use it consistently. Communication partners deliver the system in the same way. Written instructions are essential. These can be kept with the communication system and also documented in a Communication Passport. A short video may also be useful for new communication partners.

Because of the key role that the communication partner plays in this access method, it may be worth trying to ensure that the individual has a consistent set of trained communication partners when they are getting started. Once all have built confidence in the system, new communication partners can be introduced.
