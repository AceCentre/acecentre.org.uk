﻿Eye Pointing

In this chapter:

• Be introduced to eye pointing for communication

• Consider E-tran frames and alternatives

• Consider the number and placement of options when using eye pointing for communication

• Think about how a selection is made on an E-tran frame

• Reflect on the communication partner skills that are needed to support eye pointing

For individuals who are not able to point directly to symbols, but have reasonable control of their eye movements, eye pointing to symbols can be an access option to explore.

Eye pointing can simply involve a communication partner holding up two objects or symbols for the individual to look at and make a selection from. However, it can also be developed into a full and rich communication system.

Learning to look at an item and look back at the communication partner involves lots of different skills. The Vision and Communication Research Team at UCL and Great Ormond Street Hospital are active in this area - see ucl.ac.uk/gaze. Amongst other work, they have developed an eye pointing classification scale to describe and support the use of looking in communication by children with complex physical disabilities. This can be downloaded from their website.

Using eye pointing for communication is not necessarily something that everyone will be able to do immediately, and it is not an access method that is suitable for everyone. In order to build skills in eye pointing, start by using it within fun games or activities and responding to where you think the person is looking. If you use fun and motivating no-fail activities it may help to avoid the frustration that can alienate an individual from learning to use this skill. This is very similar to the approach described in the Learning to Point section above. .

If using eye pointing to purposefully communicate messages proves too difficult for an individual for whatever reason, it may be worth thinking about another access method, such as listener mediated scanning.
