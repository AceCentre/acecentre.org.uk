Ruben chats to his Mum

 

Where an individual does have a reliable way of indicating ‘yes’ and ‘no’, someone might start off using the second method, but move to the first method as both the individual and the communication partner gain confidence in the approach.

When getting started, you might describe what the individual is doing and why you are interpreting it as ‘yes’. For example, you might feedback, “You are smiling to say ‘yes, that’s the one I want’”.

In this video, Ruben is chatting to his Mum. He communicates ‘yes’ when he hears the option he wants by raising his arms and smiling. He has a large communication book full of words and phrases that he can choose from. You can see pages from a book similar to his later in the chapter. The monkey noises in the background are courtesy of a sibling playing in the same room!

Chapter 7 - Listener Mediated Scanning

Section 1 - Making a Selection

In this video, Eva is learning to use a simple one message voice output communication aid to say ‘yes’ when she hears the option that she wants. She accesses this by means of a switch which is attached to her wheelchair. She is at the early stages of learning how this works, and the switch position is still being established. Eva and her communication partner will be working on pressing the switch with the side of her head, rather than catching it with her eye.

It is helpful to accompany the chart or book with clear instructions. Unless it is very obvious, it is useful to describe how someone indicates ‘yes’ and (where relevant) ‘no’. It is also helpful to document this in their Communication Passport.
