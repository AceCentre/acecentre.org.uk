Chapter 1 - Introduction to Paper-Based AAC

Adapting Paper-Based AAC

Symbol based charts and communication books often look fairly similar. They tend to be designed to be used by someone who points to symbols to communicate alongside or instead of their speech. However, as the collection of pictures above showed, lots of adaptations and adjustments can be made to make resources work for people who can’t easily point to a symbol.

This guide will explore adaptations that can be made to support access to symbols in a variety of ways, including by pointing, visual accessibility issues, the use of eye pointing, the use of encoding, listener mediated scanning, coded access and combination access. It will also look at how to make such symbol charts and touch on how these can be developed into communication books.

Some will need a mixture of different access methods over the course of a day or to deal with different situations or environments. For example, someone might point directly to a communication book during the day then switch to listener mediated scanning when they are fatigued or unwell, or someone might have a simplified system for when they are travelling in the car. Note that if someone is using more than one type of accesss method to paper-based communication, it may affect the design of the resources. You will need to ensure that the resource can either accommodate both access methods, or, if you are using separate ones, that the resources are as consistent as possible. You may also want to think through how someone can request each tool. Information about this should be well documented, perhaps on the resource itself and / or in a Communication Passport.

As well as adapting resources to meet the needs of the individual who is communicating, do bear in mind that sometimes it will be necessary to take the needs of communication partners into account. For example, if a key communication partner in their life has a visual impairment, you may need to increase the size of font used to enable that person to see what the individual is selecting.

The focus of this guide is on access to paper-based symbol resources. Symbol based resources are used by people whose literacy levels are not sufficient to enable them to rely upon the printed word to support their face-to-face communication. Access to text based resources is explored in Designing and Using Alphabet Charts - Getting started with AAC. Note that access to alphabet charts are important in terms of developing literacy skills, even if they are not being used to support face-to-face communication at the current time.

Many individuals choose to use a mixture of paper-based and electronic AAC.

Electronic AAC, also known as high tech AAC, produces a spoken voice. This might involve a speech synthesiser, pre-recorded phrases, or a combination of the two. Electronic AAC varies in complexity from simple battery operated devices that play a single pre-recorded message through to more compex equipment such as computers, tablets and iPads. To find out more about what’s available, you could explore the database of UK suppliers of AAC produced by Communication Matters - see communicationmatters.org.uk/supplier-database. Call Centre Scotland has produced a guide to apps that are available to support communication - see callscotland.org.uk/downloads/posters-and-leaflets/ipad-apps-for-complex-communication-support-needs.

While this guide is focused on access to paper-based symbol resources, you may find that some of the information is useful when considering access to electronic AAC.
