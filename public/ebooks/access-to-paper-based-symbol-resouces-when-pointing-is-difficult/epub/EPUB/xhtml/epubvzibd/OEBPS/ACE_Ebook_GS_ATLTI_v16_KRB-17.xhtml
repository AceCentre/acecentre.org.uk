Chapter 3

﻿﻿Visual Accessibility

In this chapter:

• Consider visual difficulties that can impact upon the use of AAC

• Explore tools and strategies that can support visual access for some

• Look at tangible symbols

This chapter explores ways of enhancing the visual accessibility of paper-based charts and books. Visual difficulties can involve problems with the mechanics of seeing (acuity), but also with the processing of visual information.

Visual difficulties can have a significant impact on someone’s ability to make use of a symbol based chart or communication book. Symbols and symbol charts can be busy and detailed and visual skills play a huge part in learning what symbols mean and how to use them. However, visual difficulties can be poorly understood.

Reports from relevant professionals can be an invaluable source of information. If a child in school has support from an advisory teacher for vision impairment, they may be able to provide advice on the design of visual materials such as symbols charts.

If you are aware that someone has a visual difficulty, you might want to try and find out:

if both near and distance tasks are impacted?

if the condition is degenerative or stable?

does the condition fluctuate?

is this a new diagnosis?

does good light/poor light impact on their vision?

what other environmental/general factors impact their vision?

is there any field loss?

are any low vision aids used?

Cortical Visual Impairment can be particularly challenging, as visual skills may fluctuate and change over time. Christine Roman-Lantzy’s book Cortical Visual Impairment: An Approach to Assessment and Intervention is a useful resource. Documenting what you think someone is seeing and their visual behaviours may help to build up a clearer picture of the impact of any visual difficulties on someone’s ability to use communication materials. Chart design for someone with a cortical visual difficulty may well involve some trial and error!
