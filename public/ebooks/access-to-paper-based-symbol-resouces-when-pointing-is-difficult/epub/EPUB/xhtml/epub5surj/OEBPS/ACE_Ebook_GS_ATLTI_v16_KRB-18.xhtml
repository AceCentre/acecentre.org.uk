Colour blindness is another factor that may need to be taken into account. Although more males are affected, colour blindness can also be experienced by females. Some may have undiagnosed colour blindness, particularly since screening for this is no longer standard in the UK. If a person is found to be colour blind, this information can be used to adjust the colours used within a communication chart so that it is as visually accessible as possible. See colourblindawareness.org for more information.

Communication does, of course, involve at least two people. Therefore you may occasionally need to take the visual abilities of regular communication partners into account, as they need to be able to see what is being selected for communication to succeed.

There are lots of factors that can be varied to accommodate visual difficulties, including size of symbols / text, use of spacing on a page, type of symbol used, use of colour (both within symbols and on the page), shape and positioning of the chart. However, if any visual access to symbols is ultimately too difficult, an alternative strategy such as auditory scanning may be considered.

﻿Tools and Strategies to Support Visual Access

Use of Matt Laminate

Communication charts and book pages are often laminated to make them more robust and easier to use. Matt laminate tends to work best from a visual perspective as gloss laminate may reflect overhead lighting causing distortion..

An alternative is to print the chart or pages on so-called ‘indestructible’, tear-proof or waterproof paper.

Clear Photographs

Sometimes paper-based communication systems will be based largely around photographs. However, photographs still have a role to play in a symbol based communication system. While a symbol of ‘friend’ is ideal for talking about the concept of having a friend, when talking about a specific friend called Sally, a photograph of Sally may be more useful.

If photographs are being used, it is important to consider how clear they are as a badly taken photograph can actually be far harder to learn and use than a clearly drawn symbol. It is easier to recognise an object or a person from a photograph if the picture is taken against a clear / plain background. This is especially important when using photographs on a communication chart, as they can end up being really quite small.
