Chapter 11 - Next Steps - Developing a Communication Book

A template for a pragmatic-based auditory scan book, similar to that used by Ruben, is available on the Ace Centre website acecentre.org.uk/product-category/alphabet-books. It complements the PODD approach.

The Super Core communication book is based around the Super Core vocabulary package found within Grid 3 communication software. It is a symbol based communication book and is designed to accessed by pointing or listener mediated scanning. It is available from thinksmartbox.com.

Score is a paper and power-based vocabulary that is available from techcess.co.uk. There are communication charts and a binder which complement the Score vocabulary package in MindExpress 5 communication software. It is designed to be accessed by pointing.

Low-tech support books are available from Liberator. The books complement a range of vocabulary packages found on their electronic communication aids, including easyChat Core, LAMP Words for Life and WordPower and are designed to be accessed by pointing. The books are free to download from liberator.co.uk. Manual boards (symbol charts) to complement vocabulary packages found on their Accent devices can also be downloaded.

The Pixon Project Kit is a communication and language curriculum that is based around a set of symbols called Pixons. While not resulting in a communication book, it is another symbol based resource to be aware of. The kit consists of paper-based communication charts and teaching materials with a focus on core vocabulary. These are designed to be accessed by pointing. It is available from liberator.co.uk.

PECS® (Picture Exchange Communication System), discussed above, is a communication system that has been designed to support people who have autism or who are on the autistic spectrum and need to learn the purpose of communication. Symbols are picked up and given to communication partners. You can find out much more about PECS® at pecs-unitedkingdom.com.

Other Resources for Making a Communication Book

There are a number of freely downloadable communication book templates available on the ace centre website acecentre.org.uk. While these are largely text based, they do include templates for creating communication books based around auditory scanning, including the pragmatic-based auditory scan book discussed in the section above.

For those using the Proloquo2Go app by AssistiveWare, there are instructions on how to create a complementary paper-based communication book on their website assistiveware.com/blog/making-a-paper-based-aac-book. These could also provide inspiration for adapting other vocabulary packages within communication software.

Ability World sells lots of A5, A4 and A3 folders that can be used to make communication books. They have practical features like handles and velcro fastenings. They also have creative solutions like small books that can be worn on a belt or lanyard, waist mounted books and totes. See ability-world.com.

Easel files are available from stationery shops. The robust Look2Talk easel file can be purchased as a stand alone item from acecentre.org.uk/resources/look2talk.
