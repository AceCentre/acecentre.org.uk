Number of Symbols

As well as the use of space, how someone points will affect the number of symbols that can be displayed on a page. It is often an advantage to have more symbols on a chart. This both maximises the amount the individual is able to communicate with the chart and gives the communication partner access to more vocabulary when they are using the chart themselves (i.e. modelling - see Chapter 8 of Using Paper-Based Symbol Systems with Children - Getting started with AAC for a full discussion). It also reduces the number of page turns needed to communicate ideas.

However, the advantages of having more symbols on a chart needs to be balanced against someone’s physical and visual skills. If these mean that you cannot place enough symbols on a single page to meet their language needs, you will need to design a system that incorporates page turns and provides ways of moving easily between pages.

Alternatively, you might want to think about using encoding to increase the amount of information on a chart, or look at some of the other access methods in this guide.

Placement of Symbols on a Page

It is important to look at how someone moves as they point to items on a chart. If it is easier to access items in certain areas then this information can help you plan the placement of symbols on the chart with important and / or frequently used symbols being placed in areas that are easier to select.

For example, if someone is able to access all the symbols on their chart, but finds it easier to select the ones on the left hand side, you might place the most useful symbols on the left and put the ones used less frequently on the right. If there are areas of the page that they can’t reach at all, then these should be left blank.
