Coded charts can be set up in different ways and can be quite complex if required. The design will need to take an individual’s language needs and access abilities into account.

In this more complex example, the first chart contains the target symbols. The second chart is used by the individual to indicate the code.

This time there is a three-step process to identify the target symbols. The individual first indicates the quadrant that contains the target symbol, and then identifies its row number and column colour.

To communicate ‘different’, they would first select the bottom left quadrant on the main chart. They might do this by eye pointing to the quadrant, or by some other means such as fist pointing (if this was difficult, the quadrant areas could be added to their chart of codes, as in Angie’s below). Having identified the quadrant, they would then select ‘2’ and ‘red’ from their chart of codes, as ‘different’ is in the second row and the red column.

Using just eight locations on their chart of codes (or twelve if the quadrants are specified), they can access and use vocabulary on a chart containing sixty-four symbols.
