Help with Page Turning

When using a few communication charts or a full communication book, some people need a bit of extra help to turn the pages. One strategy is to make use of your communication partner, and have an agreed way of indicating to them that you would like them to turn the page – perhaps a special symbol on the page or a gesture or vocalisation.

Another approach is to use page spacers. These are a material stuck to the back of a page that enables someone to slide their finger into the space and flick the page over more easily. Page spacers can be created using self-adhesive foam squares, craft glue dots, the soft (female) Velcro™ dots or even the felt dots that are used on the bottom of furniture to protect floors.

Others find that sticking lolly sticks (available in packs from craft shops) to the back of each page helps them to flick the pages over.

Laminating the pages helps make them sturdier, and is definitely worth doing if you are using spacers or lolly sticks. As always, use of matt laminate is suggested as glossy laminate can reflect overhead light

Section 3 - Exchange

﻿Exchange

Exchange is a slightly different form of direct access. Exchange is a method of using paper-bassed AAC which requires the person to give a symbol to their communication partner. Typically the symbols will be laminated with Velcro™ on the back, and kept either on a chart or within a book.

The approach is termed exchange because individual symbols are handed to a communication partner in return for an item. As the system develops, a sequence of symbols can be placed on a sentence strip which is handed over. Exchange can be a useful approach for individuals who are not able to understand that pointing to symbols can be communicative.

The most well known form of exchange is PECS® (Picture Exchange Communication System). PECS® is a communication system that has been designed to support people who have autism or who are on the autistic spectrum and need to learn the purpose of communication. It is a six stage process that is widely used in the UK.

You can find out much more about PECS® at pecs-unitedkingdom.com

While PECS®, and other exchange systems, can be hugely beneficial for some, there are some practical disadvantages to such systems, particularly from an access perspective.

By placing Velcro™ on the back of each symbol, it is inevitable that the communication book becomes thicker and heavier. Unless each symbol has an identified place on the page, the symbols may move around both on a page and between pages in a communication book. This can make it harder to find a symbol, particularly for someone with a visual difficulty, and reduces opportunities for motor learning.
