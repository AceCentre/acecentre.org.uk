Special Symbols to Support Visual Difficulties

There are some symbols that have been specially designed to be easier to see.

PCS High Contrast symbols were designed by Speech Pathologists Linda Burkhart and Gayle Porter for individuals with low vision or visual impairment. The set of approximately 1450 symbols are designed to be used against a black background.

Chart Design

Alongside the choice of symbol and use of colour on the page, you may want to think about many of the issues discussed in Chart Design to Support Pointing. Factors such as how many symbols are displayed at any one time, the size of the symbols and any accompanying text, and the use of space between the cells can all impact on the chart’s visual accessibility.

Positioning

Where materials are positioned can also impact on their visual accessibility. The distance between the person and the materials is a key consideration. You may also want to consider where in their visual field someone sees best, and present the materials accordingly. Once you have determined which position is best to facilitate optimum visual access, this could be documented in their Communication Passport.

Consistency

Keeping the location of symbols on a page consistent is helpful whether or not someone has a visual difficulty as it gives the opportunity for them to start pointing to symbols using automaticity. For example, if the symbol ‘more’ is always in the top left, if someone wants to select that, their hand may start moving towards it without them having to consciously plan.It is not dissimilar to someone being able to touch type without looking at the keyboard. Where visual difficulties are involved, consistency of positioning is especially helpful as it means that someone does not have to search for an item.

If a symbol is repeated on a number of different charts or pages of a book, it is helpful to place it in the same location where possible. Similarly, ‘testing’ by swapping symbols around on a chart is best kept to a minimum. Imagine how annoying (and difficult) it would be if every time you came to type on your computer keyboard, someone had swapped the letters around!

Pick Up and Show (by the communication partner)

Pick up and show was discussed above in Chapter 2. In that context, the individual might pick up the symbol and show it if they find that movement easier than pointing. However, pick up and show materials can be used in a different way to support visual access. Here, the idea is that the communication partner picks up the symbol and holds it in a place that is easier for the individual to see.
