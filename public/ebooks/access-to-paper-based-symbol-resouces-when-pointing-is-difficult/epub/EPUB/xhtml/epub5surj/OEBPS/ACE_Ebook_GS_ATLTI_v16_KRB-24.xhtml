Section 2 - Tangible Symbols

More Abstract Tangible Symbols

While the term tangible symbols is sometimes used as an alternative to the term objects of reference, it can also be used to describe much more abstract symbols. For example, you might make a communication chart about days of the week, with different textures and shapes used to convey each day. In this context, tangible symbols are also known as tactile symbols.

By their very nature, such symbols are often homemade, as in the example below:

Project Core has developed thirty-six 3D printable symbols that can be downloaded and printed, perhaps by a company offering 3D printing services. See project-core.com/3d-symbols.

Another way of creating custom tangible symbols is to make use of a Zyfuse Heater see zychem-ltd.co.uk. This printer is designed to create tactile diagrams, but could be used to print symbols. The black ink swells on the special paper to create a diagram that can be felt using the hands. The Tactile Library website tactilelibrary.com is a free library of diagrams used in the education of people who are blind and partially sighted. The website contains links to other tactile graphic image libraries.

A 3D doodle pen could be used to create a similar effect by hand.

There is a range of Tactile Symbol Cues, produced in America, that are commercially available - see adaptivedesign.org/adaptations or acecent.re/1IRp7gv for more information. Their symbol for ‘no’ is illustrated here. The bright red tangible ‘X‘ sits on the card. The label ‘NO’ is printed for reference.

See acecent.re/1G3Xdx0 for a discussion around custom tactile symbols for individuals with cortical visual impairment. You can find out more about tactile symbols here library.tsbvi.edu/Browse/Category/15.

Tactile alphabets are also available - see the RNIB website for more information rnib.org.uk/braille-and-moon-tactile-codes. Logan Technologies sell a 6dot™ Braille Label Maker™ with both six key braille and qwerty input logan-technologies.co.uk/braille-label-maker. It is worth bearing tactile alphabets in mind, as early exposure to the alphabet is essential to support literacy development.
